INTRODUCTION

Preface

“War. War never changes.

The Romans waged war to gather slaves and wealth. Spain built an empire from its lust for gold and territory. Hitler shaped a battered Germany into an economic superpower.

But war never changes.

In the 21st century, war was still waged over the resources that could be acquired. Only this time, the spoils of war were also its weapons: Petroleum and Uranium. For these resources, China would invade Alaska, the US would annex Canada, and the European Commonwealth would dissolve into quarreling, bickering nation-states, bent on controlling the last remaining resources on Earth.

In 2077, the storm of world war had come again. In two brief hours, most of the planet was reduced to cinders. And from the ashes of nuclear devastation, a new civilization would struggle to arise.”

- Ron Perlman, Fallout Intro

Fallout: Quest is a post-nuclear RPG set sometime after a great nuclear conflict in the year 2077. 99% of humanity and a good deal of other life perished in the nuclear war. Many creatures still survived, although some were altered into mutated abominations beyond imagination. One can play Fallout: Quest either as a campaign-oriented RPG, with a series of smaller adventures in a larger story, or as a single-story game with a minimum of involvement in the larger workings of the world. It can be played on a single Saturday night with two or three friends or in a group of seven or eight two or three times a week for months on end. The only limits are scheduling and the bounds of your imagination.

Much like the popular tabletop RPG, Dungeons & Dragons, Fallout: Quest is driven by imagination. It’s about creating memorable scenarios, characters, and adventures that will be shared and recounted by you and your group of fellow players for years to come.

Unlike a game of make-believe, Fallout: Quest gives a structured way of determining the consequences of the players’ actions. Players roll dice to resolve whether their attacks hit or miss or whether their characters can convince a band of raiders to leave them alone, avoid a super mutant’s sledgehammer, or pull off some other dangerous task. Anything is possible, but the dice make some outcomes more probable than others.

In the Fallout: Quest, each player creates a character (also called a survivor or wanderer) and teams up with a group of other characters played by their friends. Working together, the group might explore a pre-war science lab, a ruined city, an irradiated cesspool, a raider boss’s hideout, or an underground vault. The wanderers can solve puzzles, converse with other characters, battle horrible abominations, and discover fabulous pre-war technology and other forgotten treasures.

One player, however, takes on the role of the Game Master (GM) also known as the Overseer. The GM acts as the game’s lead storyteller and referee. The GM creates adventures for the characters, who decide what they want to do and where they want to go. The GM determines the results of the survivors’ actions and narrates what they experience. Because the GM can improvise to react to anything the players attempt, Fallout: Quest is infinitely flexible, and each adventure can be exciting and unexpected.

There is no definitive end to Quest: Combat. Once one adventure is completed another one can immediately begin. The game also has no real winners or losers. Sometimes things don’t go as planned and a wanderer may succumb to the perils of the wasteland, however if everyone had a good time and created a memorable story, they all win.

The Wasteland

During the Great War, it took no more than two hours for the nuclear warheads that were fired to change the face of the planet and affect life on Earth for many centuries to come. After the atomic fire had cleared, the surface of the Earth was very different.

Millions of buildings and cities were demolished or severely damaged, with only a few exceptions. Most of the plants that had once flourished on the Earth died as the Earth was changed from a diverse landscape to a barren and hot desert, radiation becoming a serious threat to what little life remained.

Countries such as the United States were turned into scorched wastelands, filled with new dangers. Hazardous mutations altered the wildlife, making them more dangerous than before. As the remnants of humanity began to spread once again, new violence was introduced in the form of splintered factions. They began warring against each other in a new anarchic world. Although more than two centuries have passed since the Great War, progress in the reclamation of society has been slow and the wasteland remains a radioactive and war-torn desert, in which life itself is a struggle.

The world of Fallout is typically centered around various regions spanning the United States from New California to the Capital Wasteland, each with their own lore and local flavor. However, Fallout: Quest is not limited to these settings and your GM might want to explore previously untouched locations such as Louisiana or even China. Because there is so much diversity in the wastes of Fallout: Quest, you should check with your GM about any house rules that will affect your play of the game. Ultimately, the GM is the authority on the campaign and its setting, even if the setting is a previously established region.

Using This Guide

The Wasteland Survival Guide is broken up into three parts.

Part 1 is about creating a character, providing the rules and guidance you need to make the character you’ll play in the game.

Part 2 details the rules of how to play the game, beyond the basics described in this introduction.

Part 3 is all about equipment. It contains a collection of tables and descriptions of many of the kinds of items survivors will come across on their adventures.

Game Dice

The game uses polyhedral dice with different numbers of sides. You can find dice like these in game stores and in many bookstores. In these rules, the different dice are referred to by the letter d followed by the number of sides: d4, d6, d8, d 10, d12, and d20. For instance, a d6 is a six-sided die (the typical cube that many games use).

The D20

The d20 is the primary catalyst of fate in Fallout: Quest. When a character performs an action of which the outcome is uncertain, the player controlling that character rolls a d20. The result of the roll ultimately decides whether that character’s action succeeds or fails. Every character and enemy in the game has capabilities defined by seven ability scores. The abilities are Strength, Perception, Endurance, Charisma, Intelligence, Agility, and Luck, or SPECIAL for short. SPECIAL scores typically range from 3 to 18 for most survivors. (Creatures might have scores as low as 1 or as high as 30.) These ability scores, and the ability modifiers derived from them, are the basis for almost every d20 roll that a player makes on a character’s or enemy’s behalf.

Ability checks, attack rolls, and saving throws are the three main kinds of d20 rolls, forming the core of the rules of the game. All three follow these simple steps.

1. Roll the die and add a modifier. Roll a d20 and add the relevant modifier. This is typically the modifier derived from one of the 7 ability scores, and it sometimes includes a Tag Skil bonus to reflect a character’s particular skill.

2. Apply circumstantial bonuses and penalties. A perk, racial trait, a particular circumstance, or some other effect might give a bonus or penalty to the check.

3. Compare the total to a target number. If the total equals or exceeds the target number, the ability check, attack roll, or saving throw is a success. Otherwise, it’s a failure. The GM (or an opposing roll in the case of a contest) designates the target number and determines whether an ability check, attack roll, or saving throw succeeds or fails.

General Rules

Specific Beats General

This book contains many rules that govern how the game plays. That said, many racial traits, weapon skills, unique items, monster abilities, and other game elements break the general rules in some way, creating an exception to how the rest of the game works. Remember this: If a specific rule contradicts a general rule, the specific rule wins

Round Down

There’s one more general rule you need to know at the outset. Whenever you divide a number in the game, round down if you end up with a fraction, even if the fraction is one-half or greater.

PART 1: CHARACTER CREATION

Your character is your personal avatar in the game and your primary channel of interacting with the world. Your character is a combination of game statistics, roleplaying hooks, and your imagination. You also choose your character’s race, traits, and perks, and invent the personality, appearance, and backstory of your character.

Before building your character, think of the kind of character you would want to play. You might want to be a survivalist that specializes in living off the land and picking off opponents from afar, or perhaps a wasteland merchant that uses their silver tongue to fill their pockets full of caps. You might want to play something unconventional such as a super mutant that has dedicated their life to literature and science, or an android that emulates the tribal warrior spirit. The possibilities are endless, so who you choose to be is entirely up to you.

Chapter 1: How to Build a Character Step-by-Step

Once you have a character in mind, define the aspects of your character by following the steps laid out in this section, making decisions that reflect the character you want. Your conception of your character might evolve with each choice you make. What’s important is that you come to the table with a character you’re excited to play.

Step 1. Choose a Race

Race defines much of a character’s identity by establishing a general appearance accompanied by ability score modifiers and a unique set of racial traits. Your character’s race also contributes to their cultural background and shapes how they interact with the world and vice versa.

Detailed descriptions of each race and their racial traits can be found in the Race section.

Step 2. Describe Your Character

Once you know the basic game aspects of your character, it’s time to flesh him or her out as a person. Your character needs a name. Spend a few minutes thinking about they look like and how they behave in general terms.

Flesh out your character’s physical appearance and personality traits. Choose your character’s alignment (the moral compass that guides their decisions) and personal beliefs and ideals. Think of what goals they have and what may influence those goals. Your character’s background describes where they from, their original occupation, and their general place in the wasteland.

Step 3. Roll Ability Scores

The name "SPECIAL" is an acronym standing for the 7 ability scores used by the game system: Strength, Perception, Endurance, Charisma, Intelligence, Agility, And Luck. The various ability scores define a character’s capacity to perform most of the game’s actions.

When creating a character, for each of the 7 ability scores roll 4d6 and record the cumulative total of the highest 3 numbers. These scores can then be swapped around and allocated to each of the 7 ability scores. If you do not wish to roll for your ability scores, you may simply take the “standard set” of: 15, 14, 13, 12, 11, 10, 9.

After assigning your ability scores, determine your ability modifiers using SPECIAL Scale. To determine an ability modifier without consulting the table, subtract 10 from the ability score and then divide the result by 2 (round down). Write the modifier next to each of your scores.

A full breakdown of what the ability scores do and the SPECIAL Scale can be found in chapter 6 of this guide.

Step 4. Calculate Secondary Stats

In addition to your ability scores and their modifiers, there are an assortment of secondary statistics that will be referenced as part of the game. Your secondary stats, such as Hit Points (HP), Defense (DEF), base speed (SPD), critical strike damage (CRIT), and carry weight (WGT) are derived from your SPECIAL scores and measure different aspects of your character. There are more secondary stats than the ones listed above, but we will calculate and write these down now as they are referenced most often.

  • HP: Hit Points calculate your character’s overall health. Your total Hit Points are equal to your Endurance plus 30.
  • DEF: Defense decreases the probability of an attack hitting you. Your base defense is equal to your passive Agility (10 plus your Agility modifier).
  • SPD: Speed measures how far your character can move in feet. Your base speed is 35 feet. For every other Agility modifier, you gain or lose an additional 5 feet.
  • CRIT: You add your critical strike damage whenever you score a critical on an attack roll in combat. Your crit damage is equal to your passive Luck (10 plus your Luck modifier).
  • WGT: Carry weight measures how much you can carry in pounds. It is also related to how much you can lift, push, and throw. Your maximum carry weight is equal to your Strength multiplied by 10.

Step 5. Choose Tag Skills

The next step is to select your Tag skills. Tag skills are the skills your character excels at. Each character can select three tag skills at the start of the game. You start off with a Tag skill bonus of +1 which gets added on to your roll whenever you use one of your Tag skills. Your Tag skill bonus will increase as you level-up.

Step 6. Choose Starting Traits

When creating your character, you can select up to two special starting traits from the traits section. These traits have unique benefits and drawbacks and can only be chosen at the beginning of the game. You do not have to select any traits if you don’t want to, but you will not be able to acquire any of the starting traits later on in the game.

Step 7. Select Starting Equipment

When you create your character, you start with a number of caps at the start of the game to spend on items found in the item lists. You start off with a number of caps equal to 4d6 plus your Charisma and Luck modifiers plus your Barter bonus.

You decide how your character came by this starting equipment. It might have been an inheritance, or goods that the character purchased during their upbringing. You might have been equipped with a weapon, armor, and a backpack as part of NCR military service. You might even have stolen your gear from a fellow wanderer.

Step 8. Come Together and Play

Most survivors don’t work alone. Each character plays a role within a party, a group of wanderers working together for a common purpose. Teamwork and cooperation greatly improve your party’s chances to survive the many perils in the wasteland. Talk to your fellow players and your GM to decide whether your characters know one another, how they met, and what sorts of quests the group might undertake.

Chapter 2: Race

After the events of the Great War, 99% of all life was extinguished from the earth, however mankind survived. Along with them a few humanoid species remain. Small remnants of mankind’s machinations survived the nuclear holocaust and others arose from its wake. The four playable races in Fallout: Quest are Android, Ghoul, Human, and Super Mutant.

Your character’s race not only affects your ability scores and racial traits but also provides the cues for building your character’s story. Each race’s description in this section includes information to help you roleplay a character of that race, including personality, physical appearance, features of society, and racial alignment tendencies. These details are suggestions to help you think about your character; wanderers can deviate widely from the norm for their race. It’s worthwhile to consider why your character is different, as a helpful way to think about your character’s background and personality.

Ghoul

Ghouls, necrotic post-humans, are decrepit, rotting, zombie-like mutants. They are recipients of intense and prolonged radiation sickness which decays their skin, and in some cases their brains. Their skin hangs off their bones, sometimes in shreds, and sunken eyes peer out from skulls twisted and burned by radiation, thus giving these unfortunate people their name. Their skin can be anywhere from pale white to dirt brown in color, with green and yellow the most common. When they do have hair remaining, it usually does not grow.

Prejudice and Bigotry

Ghouls are turned away from most human communities and are prejudiced against due to their outward appearance and the possibility of going feral. Humans commonly refer to ghouls as “shamblers” or “zombies,” and more often shoot first and ask questions later when they encounter them in the wastes. Ghouls live most of their lives in social isolation or within groups of other ghouls.

Going Feral

All ghouls eventually go feral when their brains rot, and their mental faculties are reduced to an animal-like state. Exposure to radiation seems to exacerbate the process even though it is known to benefit ghouls in low doses. Feral ghouls travel in packs and attack without forethought. They can often be found feasting on the remains of survivors unfortunate enough to cross their path.

Ghoul Traits

SPECIAL Modifiers.

  • -1 Strength
  • +2 Endurance
  • -2 Charisma
  • +2 Luck

Age. Ghouls enjoy an extremely slow cellular mitosis rate, rendering them essentially immune to the effects of old age but also leaving them sterile.

Alignment. Ghouls are often on the chaotic side. The tendency to shun ghouls away from typical social orders has left them resentful of laws and authority.

Crippling Addiction. Ghouls are especially susceptible to jet (a popular wasteland chem) addiction. Ghouls always roll with disadvantage on addiction saves when taking jet.

Darkvision. As a side-effect of ghoulification, ghouls have superior vision in dark and dim conditions. Ghouls can see in dim light within 60 feet of them as if it were bright light, and in darkness as if it were dim light. They can't discern color in darkness, only shades of gray.

Healing Glow. Ghouls are not only immune to radiation but actually heal from it. Ghouls heal HP for each rad-level they take.

Slow Metabolism. Ghouls have a natural resistance to all chems, and thus when taking chems, ghouls only receive a fraction of their effects. Chems last one round shorter (but not less than one) for ghouls and stimpaks only heal half the usual HP. Ghouls are also resistant to bleeding, disease, and poison.

Human

Humans, or homo sapiens (sometimes called smoothskins or normals by ghouls and super mutants, respectively) are a species that have been more-or-less unaffected by radiation. In post-nuclear America, four major ethnic groups have so far been distinguished: African, Asian, Caucasian, and Hispanic. Evolved and mutated humans, while still members of the homo genus, are a different species from regular humans.

Rebuilding the Wasteland

Humans desperately cling to the world they ruined even centuries after its destruction. Humans have set up small settlements across the wasteland in hopes of reclaiming some of the old world’s former glory and shielding themselves from the horrors of the wastes.

Merchants and caravans routinely travel from settlement to settlement, braving the arid sands and hostile creatures of the wastes to sell recovered trinkets of the old world and new commodities alike. Others have turned to banditry and raiding in order to survive, believing that in a world without order only violence and power will rule.

Vault Dwellers

While most humans are the descendants of above-ground survivors of the Great War’s aftermath, others are the children of survivors locked away in underground fallout shelters called vaults. Officially, the vaults were designed for the sole purpose of sheltering up to one thousand citizens in the event of a nuclear holocaust; however, in reality, they were a series of secret experiments orchestrated by Vault-Tec and the United States government. The experiments ranged from genetically modifying the genome of the vault’s inhabitants to locking away a single man solely accompanied by a box of puppets. Vault dwellers do not typically last long in the wasteland, but those that do prove to be formidable survivors.

Human Traits

SPECIAL Modifiers.

  • +1 SPECIAL of choice

Age. Humans reach adulthood in their late teens and live less than a century.

Alignment. Humans tend toward no particular alignment. The best and the worst are found among them.

Sensitive to Radiation. Humans are not immune to the radioactive elements rife throughout the wasteland. Exposure to radiation imposes detrimental effects on humans called radiation sickness. If a human is exposed to too much radiation they may become incapacitated, die, or worse, become a ghoul. More details on the effects of radiation sickness can be found in the radiation section in chapter 7.

Super Mutant

Super mutants are mutated humans, products of infection by the Forced Evolutionary Virus (FEV). They are much taller, bulkier and muscular than pure strain humans, have (mostly) green, gray, or yellowish skin, are immune to disease and radiation, and are gifted with superhuman strength and endurance. Although they are completely sterile, the rapid regeneration of their cells caused by FEV makes them virtually biologically immortal (but not immune to death from injury).

Unity in Superiority

Like ghouls, mutants are largely outcast from human society. Many of them prefer it this way, looking on human society as diseased or inferior because of the prejudice and corruption that still exist

West Coast vs. East Coast

West Coast super mutants were once part of a great army, united under a single Master. Under the Master, mutants sought to convert the purest wastelanders into their ranks in an effort to rid the wastes of the scourge of humanity. After the death of the Master, mutants were scattered and disorganized, banding together in loose groups with little order or structure.

East Coast super mutants were bred in Vault 87 with a modified strain of FEV that further changed their biology. Vault 87 mutants grew larger and less intelligent with age, allowing them to grow from eight (2.44 m) to at least twenty feet (6 m) in height; the largest are known as "behemoths." These behemoths seem to be even less intelligent than their smaller brethren, talking only in echoing roars and blindly destroying everything in their path. Behemoths act more like unfettered beasts than soldiers.

Super Mutant Traits

SPECIAL Modifiers.

  • +2 Strength
  • +2 Endurance
  • -1 Charisma
  • -2 Intelligence

Age. While they will not normally die due to the normal effects of aging, super mutants are prone to senility, leading to tendencies to engage in near-psychotic, aggressive attacks against humans and other creatures.

Alignment. Super mutants are usually hostile to anyone or anything that is not another mutant. There are, however, mutants that are docile and cohabitate with humans, either due to an uncommonly friendly demeaner or outright senility.

Bulky. Because of their bulky exterior, mutants suffer -3 defense and can only wear armor designed for mutants.

FEV Effects. Super mutants gain +4 maximum HP and are immune to disease and radiation as a result of their exposure to the Forced Evolutionary Virus.

Ham-Fisted. Due to their massive, sausage-like fingers, super mutants suffer -3 on attack rolls with ranged weapons and cannot use pistols or SMGs.

Synth

Synth is a diminutive for “synthetic humanoid” and is used to name synthetic intelligence units designed by the Institute to look, function and behave like humans. They are sometimes also called androids or artificial humans.

Generational Complexity

Synths are classified into one of three generations depending on their complexity:

Generation 1 synths are purely robotic androids, possessing all the physical capabilities of a human, but being no smarter than a standard protectron. They resemble a lanky skeleton with metal joints, mechanical "organs" and hydraulic muscles. They are completely loyal to the Institute and speak in a high-pitched, robotic tone. Like all robots, they can take large amounts of damage, never backing down from a fight even with their limbs blown off.

Generation 2 synths are similar in appearance to their predecessors but are hardier and sheathed in plastic "flesh" mounted on a thin metal lattice surrounding their mechanical skeleton. Though more human-like in appearance, Gen-2s still possess limited A.I. and superficially resemble a mannequin more than a human, having visible seams along their sickly, yellow-gray skin. Their skin is fragile however and will fall off in large chunks if struck or shot with enough force to reveal their mechanical insides. They also speak in a robotic voice, but at a much lower pitch than Gen 1 synths.

Generation 3 synths represent the pinnacle of synth technology, being virtually indistinguishable from natural-born humans down to the cellular level. Each Gen 3 synth is built from lab-grown bones, muscles and other tissues that are assembled and brought to life at the Institute's Robotics lab and are "born" with the bodies and mental faculties of full-grown adult humans. Though entirely biological, each Gen 3 synth contains a neurological implant inside their brain allowing them to be "programmed" and manipulated via voice commands. This implant cannot be detected nor removed without killing the synth.

Slavery and Freedom

The synths' Institute creators view them as nothing more than machines and treat them as slaves. The Railroad has committed itself to freeing Generation 3 synths from their masters and smuggling them out of the Commonwealth to somewhere the Institute cannot reach them. In contrast, the Brotherhood of Steel has sworn to eliminate all synths, for to them, a machine capable of thinking like a human is an abomination of technology.

Synth Traits

SPECIAL Modifiers.

  • +2 Perception
  • +2 Intelligence
  • -2 Agility
  • -1 Luck

Age. Generation 1 and 2 synths do not age but do break down as their parts corrode over time; generation 3 synths however age just like humans.

Alignment. Early-gen Synths seek to serve their masters and so typically have an alignment of lawful neutral. Generation 3 synths and other prototypes have free will and thus can have any alignment.

Metal Frame. Due to their nonorganic metallic construction, synths have a base DT of 5. Armor doesn’t stack on top of this base but serves as an overall minimum DT. They also are immune to disease, bleed, poison, and radiation, and are also fire resistant.

Scrap Repair. Although synths cannot heal through the use of aid items, synths can use scrap metal found throughout the wasteland to repair minor damage. If a synth has scrap metal, they can use it to heal +1d6 HP plus their Repair bonus at the cost of a Major action.

Sensitive Circuitry. Synths are especially susceptible to pulse damage and thus take 1d12 damage and are staggered when inflicted with pulse.

Synthetic Biology. Early-gen synths are not composed of organic materials and so cannot reap the benefits of using chems, food, or aid items.

Chapter 3: Personality & Background

Characters are defined by much more than a collection of perks and game statistics. They’re individuals with their own stories, interests, connections, and capabilities beyond those that stats and race define. This chapter touches on the details that distinguish characters from one another, including the basics of physical description, the variety of backgrounds, and the finer points of personality and alignment.

Character Details

Your character’s name and physical description might be the first things that the other players at the table learn about you. It’s worth thinking about how these characteristics reflect the character you have in mind.

Name

Think carefully about what you want to be called. A name can reflect a great deal about the type of character someone is playing and give insight into who they are and what they’re about. If you can’t immediately think of a name, flesh out some other aspects of your character and come back to it later.

Sex

You can play a male or female character without gaining any special benefits or hindrances. In the wasteland, the norms of sexual behavior are a thing of the old-world, so you can express your sexual identity however you please.

Physical Characteristics

You choose your character’s age and the color of their hair, eyes, and skin. To add a touch of distinctiveness, you might want to give your character an unusual or memorable physical characteristic, such as a scar, a limp, or a tattoo. Physical characteristics often come with a story, so think carefully about how your character looks in conjunction to their personality and history.

Alignment

Are you a do-gooder who wants to maintain social order in the wasteland or a conniving evil-doer who just wants to see the world burn? Perhaps you are somewhere in-between. A Character’s general moral and personal attitudes are represented by their alignment. The matrix works on two axes: lawful vs chaotic and good vs evil.

Alignment is a tool for developing your character's identity. It is not a straitjacket for restricting your character. Each alignment represents a broad range of personality types or personal philosophies, so two characters of the same alignment can still be very different from each other. In addition, few people are completely consistent.

When choosing an alignment for your character, you should typically pick something on either neutral or good spectrum. Unless your GM is running a campaign in which all the characters are evil, playing an evil character in a neutral to good party disrupts group cohesion and, frankly, makes all the other players angry at you.

Personality & Characteristics

Fleshing out your character’s personality — the array of traits, mannerisms, habits, beliefs, and flaws that give a person a unique identity — will help you bring them to life as you play the game. Four categories of characteristics are presented here: personality traits, ideals, bonds, and flaws. Beyond those categories, think about your character’s favorite words or phrases, tics and habitual gestures, vices and pet peeves, and whatever else you can imagine.

Personality Traits

Give your character at least two personality traits. Personality traits are small, simple ways to help you set your character apart from every other character. Your personality traits should tell you something interesting and fun about your character. They should be self-descriptions that are specific about what makes your character stand out. “I’m smart” is not a good trait, because it describes a lot of characters. “I’ve read every book by William Shakespeare” tells you something specific about your character’s interests and disposition. Personality traits might describe the things your character likes, their past accomplishments, things your character dislikes or fears, your character’s self-attitude or mannerisms, or the influence of their ability scores.

Ideals

Describe at least one ideal that drives your character. Your ideals are the things that you believe in most strongly, the fundamental moral and ethical principles that compel you to act as you do. Ideals encompass everything from your life goals to your core belief system. Ideals might answer any of these questions: What are the principles that you will never betray? What would prompt you to make sacrifices? What drives you to act and guides your goals and ambitions? What is the single most important thing you strive for? You can choose any ideals you like, but your character’s alignment is a good place to start defining them.

Bonds

Create at least one bond for your character. Bonds represent a character’s connections to people, places, and events in the world. They tie you to things from your background. They might inspire you to heights of heroism or lead you to act against your own best interests if they are threatened. They can work very much like ideals, driving a character’s motivations and goals.

Bonds might answer any of these questions: Whom do you care most about? To what place do you feel a special connection? What is your most treasured possession?

Your bonds might be tied to your background, your race, or some other aspect of your character’s history or personality. You might also gain new bonds over the course of your adventures.

Flaws

Finally, choose at least one flaw for your character. Your character’s flaw represents some vice, compulsion, fear, or weakness — in particular, anything that someone else could exploit to bring you to ruin or cause you to act against your best interests.

More significant than negative personality traits, a flaw might answer any of these questions: What enrages you? What’s the one person, concept, or event that you are terrified of? What are your vices?

Inspiration

Inspiration is a rule the Overseer can use to reward you for playing your character in a way that’s true to their personality traits, ideal, bond, and flaw. You can gain inspiration by calling on your bond to the defense of your home village to defeat a super mutant war chief, engaging in your gambling vice and lose in a game you know is rigged, or reprimanding a town guard for abusing a beggar because of your ideal of compassion.

Gaining Inspiration

Your GM can choose to give you inspiration for a variety of reasons. Typically, GMs award it when you play out your personality traits, give in to the drawbacks presented by a flaw or bond, and otherwise portray your character in a compelling way. Your GM will tell you how you can earn inspiration in the game.

You either have inspiration or you don’t — you can’t stockpile multiple “inspirations” for later use.

Using Inspiration

If you have inspiration, you can expend it when you make an attack roll, saving throw, or ability check. Spending your inspiration gives you advantage on that roll regardless of additional circumstances.

Additionally, if you have inspiration, you can reward another player for good roleplaying, clever thinking, or simply doing something exciting in the game. When another player character does something that really contributes to the story in a fun and interesting way, you can give up your inspiration to give that character inspiration.

Backgrounds

Every story has a beginning. Your character’s background reveals where you came from, how you became a wanderer, and your place in the wasteland.

Customizing Your Background

What you did before you joined your party of wanderers is entirely up to you. Were you part of a village that was raided by bandits? Were you part the member of The Brotherhood of Steel before being excommunicated? Perhaps you were simple wasteland thief trying to make a living selling stolen wares?

Let your ability scores, Tag skills, and personality traits complement the background you choose in order to make a well-rounded, three-dimensional character.

Chapter 4: Starting Traits

Starting traits – or just traits – modify aspects of gameplay, granting a benefit in one area at the cost of hampering another. Traits can only be chosen at character creation and cannot be modified or added to your character later.

Each trait modifies (and hampers) your character’s statistics in some way; this can include SPECIAL scores, secondary stats, and skills. When making your character, you may choose a maximum of two (with a minimum of zero) traits.

Some traits are exclusive to certain races. For example, a synth could not have the Vat Skin trait and a human could not have the Glowing One trait.

Chapter 5: Leveling-Up & Perks

As your character goes on adventures and completes quests, they gain experience, represented by experience points. When your character racks up enough experience points (XP), they reach a new degree in their capabilities. This advancement is called leveling-up.

When your character levels up, they gain +1 maximum HP and get to select a perk, granting them special bonuses and abilities. As your character levels-up they might get access to new perks that were not previously available to them at lower levels. In addition to a perk, your character’s Tag skill bonus increases every 5 levels.

Tiers of Play

The shading in the Character Advancement table shows the four tiers of play. The tiers don’t have any rules associated with them other than the Tag skill bonus increase; they are a general description of how the play experience changes as characters gain levels.

In the first tier (levels 1-5), characters are effectively novice wanderers. They are learning the basics of their perks and abilities. The threats they face are relatively minor, usually posing a danger to small villages.

In the second tier (levels 6-10), characters come into their own. Characters have begun to specialize into their particular niches and have gained a decent amount of helpful equipment. These characters have become important, facing dangers that threaten wasteland towns and cities.

In the third tier (levels 11-15), characters have reached a level of power that sets them high above the ordinary populace and makes them special even among adventuring survivors. Characters have gained enough perks and equipment to be especially formidable. These mighty survivors often confront threats to whole regions.

At the fourth tier (levels 16-20), characters achieve the pinnacle of their strength, becoming heroic (or villainous) archetypes in their own right. They have fleshed out their perks and gained unique weapons and equipment. The fate of the wasteland or even the world might hang in the balance during their adventures.

Characters can keep leveling past level 20 if the GM allows it. To calculate the experience required for any level past 20, use the following formula: (n2/2) x 1,000 XP, where n stands for the level. No new perks are offered past level 20, but characters can continue picking up perks that they had skipped out on up to this point. A Character’s Tag skill bonus also keeps growing every 5 levels past 20.

Perks

Whenever your character levels up they gain a new special ability, or perk. Perks range from active skills with cooldowns to passive abilities and other effects. Each perk can only be taken once.

Before acquiring certain perks, you must meet a set of perk requirements. Requirements can range from level, SPECIAL, race, other perks, or some other additional requirements.

Perk requirements referring to your SPECIAL scores are measured by their natural values. So, if you have an item or buff that temporarily raises your Strength score from 13 to 14, you could not acquire perks that require 14 Strength. The same works in the opposite direction.

If you acquire a perk and then lose its requisites later on, you get to keep the perk. For example, a character with the Better Criticals perk would not lose the effects of the perk if their Luck fell below 12.

Combat Perk List

You can gain new abilities for your weapon or combat style of choice by taking combat perks. Much like active perks, combat perks have action costs and cooldowns. Combat perks sometimes have specific requirements but never level requirements. Combat perks relating to weapons can only be used with the weapons of their respective class. Combat perks come in one of two varieties: combat perk kits and combat kit upgrades.

Combat perk kits offer several perks for the price of one, giving characters the essential abilities for a style of combat or a particular weapon class. Before you can pick up combat perk kits of a particular class, you must first take its basic kit, i.e. you must take the basic gun kit before getting the pistol kit.

Combat kit upgrades allow you to improve your favorite combat perks and abilities (or even gain completely new ones) to further specialize your character. Unlike combat kits, upgrades can only be taken one at a time and can only be taken if you have already acquired their respective combat kit.

Chapter 6: Ability Scores (SPECIAL)

Ability scores (also known as SPECIAL scores) define a character’s capabilities, strengths, and weaknesses. The name "SPECIAL" is an acronym standing for the 7 ability scores used by the game system: Strength, Perception, Endurance, Charisma, Intelligence, Agility, And Luck. The various ability scores define a character’s capacity to perform most of the game’s actions. The three main rolls of the game — the ability check, the saving throw, and the attack roll — rely on the 7 ability scores.

This guide ’s introduction describes the basic rule behind these rolls: roll a d20, add an ability modifier derived from one of the 7 ability scores, and compare the total to a target number. This chapter focuses on how to use ability checks and saving throws, covering the fundamental activities that creatures attempt in the game.

  • Strength [STR] measures your character’s ability to carry objects and fight with melee weapons, as well as their raw physical power.
  • Perception [PER] measures your character’s ability to use their five senses, investigate, and aim with ranged weapons.
  • Endurance [END] measures your character’s stamina, physical toughness, ability to survive, and resistance to disease.
  • Charisma [CHA] measures your character’s ability to speak and interact with other characters.
  • Intelligence [INT] measures your character’s mental acuity and ability to reason, as well as their proficiency with medicine and technology.
  • Agility [AGL] measures your character’s dexterity and reflexes, as well as their ability to move on their feet and sneak.
  • Luck [LCK] measures your character’s critical attack power, as well as their chances of success in gambling or situations where no other ability score applies.

Each ability also has a modifier, derived from the score and ranging from 5 (for an ability score of 1) to +10 (for a score of 30). Because ability modifiers affect almost every attack roll, ability check, and saving throw, ability modifiers come up in play more often than their associated scores.

Advantage and Disadvantage

Sometimes a perk, weapon skill, or environmental factor will dictate that your roll has advantage or disadvantage. When that happens, you roll a second d20. Use the higher roll if the you have advantage and use the lower if you have disadvantage.

If multiple situations grant advantage or disadvantage a Character does not roll more than one additional d20.

If circumstances cause you to have both advantage and disadvantage, the roll is considered to have neither of them, and you only roll a single d20. This is true even if multiple circumstances impose disadvantage and only one grants advantage or vice versa.

Ability Checks

An ability check tests a character’s or enemy’s innate talent and training in an effort to overcome a challenge. The GM calls for an ability check when a character or monster attempts an action (other than an attack) that has a chance of failure. When the outcome is uncertain, the dice determine the results. For every ability check, the GM decides which of the 7 SPECIAL scores is relevant to the task at hand and the difficulty of the task, represented by a Difficulty Class (DC). The more difficult a task, the higher its DC. The Typical Difficulty Classes table shows the most common DCs.

To make an ability check, roll a d20 and add the relevant ability modifier. As with other d20 rolls, apply bonuses and penalties, and compare the total to the DC. If the total equals or exceeds the DC, the ability check is a success — the creature overcomes the challenge at hand. Otherwise, it’s a failure, which means the character or creature makes no progress toward the objective or makes progress combined with a setback determined by the DM.

Contests

Sometimes one character’s efforts are directly opposed to another’s. This can occur when both are trying to do the same thing and only one can succeed, such as attempting to snatch up a disarmed weapon that has fallen on the floor. This situation also applies when one of them is trying to prevent the other one from accomplishing a goal — for example, when a mutant tries to force open a door that a survivor is desperately holding closed. In situations like these, the outcome is determined by a special form of ability check, called a contest. Both participants in a contest make ability checks appropriate to their efforts. They apply all appropriate bonuses and penalties, but instead of comparing the total to a DC, they compare the totals of their two checks. The participant with the higher check total wins the contest. That character or mutant either succeeds at the action or prevents the other one from succeeding. If the contest results in a tie, the character that first initiated the action wins.

Critical Rolls

Critical rolls, also known as crits, occur when you roll one of the two extremes of the d20 (1 or 20) on an ability check, attack roll, or saving throw. Crits increase the impact of success or failure. Crits are unique in that they must be rolled naturally and cannot be modified into or out of.

When you roll a natural 20 on your d20 you score a critical success and perform outside the normal realm of possibility for your character. In combat this means you add your critical strike damage to your attack damage. Outside of combat, a critical success provides additional benefits to the action your character is trying to perform outside of being successful. For example, if you roll a crit success when trying to hold off a monster from opening a door, you might slam the door shut with such force that it breaks the opening mechanism and traps the monster on the other side.

When you roll a natural 1 on your d20 you score a critical failure. Crit fails are the exact opposite of crit success in that they automatically result in catastrophic or even comedic failure. Using the same example as before, if you roll a crit success when trying to hold off a monster from opening a door, the monster might bash through the door with such force that you are knocked down and pinned by the monster without an opportunity to react.

The effects of critical successes and failures are not always defined, so it is up to your GM to decide the effects in certain scenarios.

The Crit Threshold

Traits, perks, items, or abilities may change your crit threshold, thus modifying the range of numbers that trigger a crit. Lowering the crit threshold by 1 would reduce the requirement for a crit success to 19 and lowering it again would reduce the requirement to 18. The same goes for raising the crit fail threshold but in the opposite direction.

Tag Skills

Tag skills are your character’s specific specializations. Each skill represents a specific aspect of an ability score, and your Tag skills represent your focus on that aspect. A well-rounded character usually tags at least one diplomatic and one combat skill. A fighter will Tag combat skills whereas a diplomat may choose speech, barter and science.

Characters have a Tag skill bonus determined by level. Creatures and enemies also have this bonus, which is incorporated in their stat blocks. The bonus is used in the rules on ability checks, saving throws, and attack rolls involving skills that you’ve tagged.

Your Tag skill bonus can’t be added to a single die roll or other number more than once. For example, if two different rules say you can add your Tag Skill bonus to an Intelligence saving throw, you nevertheless add the bonus only once when you make the save.

Passive Checks

A passive check is a special kind of ability check that doesn’t involve any die rolls. Such a check can represent the average result for a task done repeatedly, such as searching for secret doors over and over again or can be used when the GM wants to secretly determine whether the characters succeed at something without rolling dice, such as noticing a hidden monster.

Here’s how to determine a character’s total for a passive check: 10 + all modifiers that normally apply to the check. If the character has advantage on the check, add 5. For disadvantage, subtract 5.

The game refers to a passive check total as a passive score. For example, if a character has a Perception of 15 they have a passive Perception of 12.

Working Together

Sometimes two or more characters team up to attempt a task. The character who’s leading the effort — or the one with the highest ability modifier — can make an ability check with advantage, reflecting the help provided by the other characters (in combat, this requires using the Help action). A character can only provide help if the task is one that he or she could attempt alone. For example, trying to pick a lock requires proficiency with thieves’ tools, so a character who lacks that proficiency can’t help another character in that task. Moreover, a character can help only when two or more individuals working together would actually be productive. Some tasks, such as threading a needle, are no easier with help.

Group Checks

When a number of characters are trying to accomplish something as a group, the GM might ask for a group ability check. In such a situation, the characters who are skilled at a particular task help cover those who aren't. To make a group ability check, everyone in the group makes the ability check. If at least half the group succeeds, the whole group succeeds. Otherwise, the group fails. Group checks don’t come up very often, and they’re most useful when all the characters succeed or fail as a group. For example, when survivors are navigating a radioactive cesspool, the GM might call for a group Survival (Endurance) check to see if the characters can avoid radiation pockets, sinkholes, and other natural hazards of the environment. If at least half the group succeeds, the successful characters are able to guide their companions out of danger. Otherwise, the group stumbles into one of these hazards.

Using SPECIAL Scores

Every task that a character or creature might attempt in the game is covered by one of the 7 ability scores. This section explains in more detail what those abilities mean and the ways they are used in the game.

Strength [STR]

Strength measures your character’s ability to carry objects and fight with melee weapons, as well as their raw physical power.

Strength Checks

A Strength check can model any attempt to lift, push, pull, or break something, to force your body through a space, or to otherwise apply brute force to a situation. The Melee Weapons and Unarmed skills are covered under Strength.

Melee Weapons. Melee Weapon checks cover whenever you attempt to use your melee weapon either inside or outside of combat. Examples include attacking a creature with a combat knife or knocking a door down with your super sledge.

Unarmed. Unarmed checks cover whenever you attempt to use your unarmed weapon either inside or outside of combat. Examples include fighting in a bare-knuckle boxing match or punching a hole through a wall with a powerfist.

Other Strength Checks. The GM might also call for a Strength check when you try to accomplish tasks like the following:

  • Force open a stuck, locked, or barred door
  • Climb, swim jump, or perform an athletic feat
  • Break free of bonds
  • Push through a tunnel that is too small
  • Hang on to a car bumper while being dragged behind it
  • Tip over a statue
  • Keep a boulder from rolling
Melee Weapon Attack Rolls and Damage.

You add your Strength modifier to your attack roll and add your Strength modifier to your base damage when attacking with a melee or unarmed weapon such as a spear or gauntlet. You use melee and unarmed weapons to make melee attacks in hand-to-hand combat, and some of them can be thrown to make a ranged attack.

Lifting, Carrying and Throwing

Carrying Capacity. Your carrying capacity is your Strength score multiplied by 10. This is the weight (measured in pounds) that you can carry. If you carry weight in to your carrying capacity you become over-encumbered, which means your speed drops to 5 feet and you have disadvantage on ability checks, attack rolls, and saving throws that use Strength, Endurance, or Agility.

Push, Drag, or Lift. You can push, drag, or lift a weight in pounds up to twice your carrying capacity (or 20 times your Strength score). While pushing or dragging weight in excess of your carrying capacity, you become over-encumbered.

Throwing Distance. You can throw objects that are one size smaller than you or smaller a distance in feet up to half your carrying capacity (or 5 times your Strength score). For every size larger divide this number by two.

Size and Strength. Larger creatures can bear more weight, whereas Tiny creatures can carry less. For each size category above Medium, double the creature’s carrying capacity and the amount it can push, drag, or lift. For a Tiny creature, halve these weights.

Perception [PER]

Perception measures your character’s ability to use their five senses, investigate, and aim with ranged weapons.

Perception Checks

A Perception check measures your general awareness of your surroundings and the keenness of your senses. A Perception check might come into play when you attempt to use any one of your five senses: sight, smell, hearing, taste, and touch to detect or otherwise identify the presence of something. The Energy Weapons and Gun Skills reflect your aptitude with ranged weapons using Perception.

Energy Weapons. Energy Weapons checks measure your proficiency with laser, plasma, and other energy-based weapons. The GM might call for an Energy Weapons (Perception) check when shooting a creature with your plasma pistol or attempting to light a campfire with a blast of your laser rifle.

Guns. Guns checks measure your aptitude and skill with guns and other projectile-based ranged weapons. You might use your Guns skill when shooting a creature with your 10mm pistol or firing a mortar.

Other Perception Checks. The GM might call for a Perception check when you try to accomplish tasks like the following:

  • Detecting a lie
  • Finding a hidden object or door
  • Discerning whether a creature is alive or dead
  • Searching for traps or ambushes
Ranged Weapon Attack Rolls

You add your Perception modifier to your attack roll when attacking with a ranged weapon, such as an energy weapon, gun, or bow.

Endurance [END]

Endurance measures your character’s stamina, physical toughness, ability to survive, and resistance to disease.

Endurance Checks

Endurance checks measure your wherewithal and general fortitude. An endurance check may come into play when you attempt to brave through a radiation storm, fight against the effects of chem withdrawal, or survive without food and water. There are no skills related to Endurance, but the GM might call for an Endurance check when you try to accomplish tasks like the following:

  • Hold your breath
  • March or labor for hours without rest
  • Go without sleep
Hit Points

Your Hit Points (HP) measure your character’s overall health and you lose Hit Points when your character is hurt or wounded in combat. Your maximum HP is equal to your Endurance score plus 30.

Charisma [CHA]

Charisma measures your character’s ability to speak and interact with other characters. It includes such factors as confidence and eloquence, and it can represent a charming or commanding personality.

Charisma Checks

A Charisma check might arise when you try to influence or entertain others, when you try to make an impression or tell a convincing lie, or when you are navigating a tricky social situation. The Barter and Speech skills measure different aspects of your charismatic capabilities.

Barter. The Barter skill helps you haggle with shopkeepers and traveling merchants. When buying or selling wares, the GM might ask for a Barter check to see if whoever you are buying from or selling to will accept the proposed deal.

Speech. Speech checks come into play whenever you attempt to deceive, intimidate, or persuade another character. Some of examples of this would be using a disguise, pry information from a captive, or convince a warlord to leave a settlement out of his warpath.

Other Charisma Checks. The GM might call for a Charisma check when you try to accomplish tasks like the following:

  • Find the best person to talk to for news, rumors, and gossip
  • Blend into a crowd to get the sense of key topics of conversation
  • Delight an audience with music, dance, acting, storytelling, or some other form of entertainment.

Intelligence [INT]

Intelligence measures your character’s mental acuity and ability to reason, as well as their proficiency with medicine and technology.

Intelligence Checks

An Intelligence check comes into play when you need to draw on logic, education, memory, or deductive reasoning. The Explosives, Medicine, Repair, Science, and Survival skills reflect different aspects of Intelligence.

Explosives. The Explosive skill measures your aptitude with explosive weapons and munitions. Explosives checks might come into play when cooking a grenade, cutting the right wire to disarm a bomb, or planting a mine.

Medicine. Medicine checks measure your ability to heal wounds and use medical supplies. You might need to make a Medicine check when attempting to diagnose and treat a disease or tend to a particularly grievous gash or wound.

Repair. Whenever you need to repair a broken item, craft a new one, or otherwise put things together. The Repair skill may come into play when crafting ammunition or working from a set of schematics.

Science. Science checks are required when working with new and old-world technology. Tasks such as hacking a terminal, working with lab equipment, or crafting microfusion cells for an energy weapon will make use of the Science skill.

Survival. The Survival skill comes into play whenever you attempt to craft chems or other consumable items from materials found around the wasteland. The GM might also ask you to make a Survival check to follow tracks, predict the weather, avoid natural hazards, or otherwise rely on knowledge of the environment.

Other Intelligence Checks. The GM might call for an Intelligence check when you try to accomplish tasks like the following:

  • Recall lore or other historical events
  • Look around for and investigate clues
  • Communicate with a creature without using words
  • Estimate the value of an item
  • Forge a document
Energy Weapon Damage

You add your Intelligence modifier to the base damage of energy weapons such as laser, plasma, or tesla weapons.

Agility [AGL]

Agility measures your character’s dexterity and reflexes, as well as their ability to move on their feet and sneak.

Agility Checks

An Agility check can model any attempt to move nimbly, quickly, or quietly, or to keep from falling on tricky footing. The Lockpick and Sneak skills make use of different aspects of Agility.

Lockpick. The Lockpick skill measures your acuity with cracking locks. The Lockpick skill may come into play when cracking a locked safe, ammunition box, or cell door.

Sneak. Make a Sneak check when you attempt to conceal yourself from enemies, slink past guards, slip away without being noticed, or sneak up on someone without being seen or heard.

Other Agility Checks. The GM might call for an Agility check when you try to accomplish tasks like the following:

  • Vault over waist-high cover
  • Disable a trap
  • Securely tie up a prisoner
  • Wriggle free of bonds
  • Hide from an enemy
Attack Rolls and Damage

You add your Agility modifier to your attack roll and add your Agility score to your base damage when attacking a melee weapon that has the finesse property, such as a shiv, dagger, or a rapier.

Defense

Your passive agility score composes your base defense. If an enemy’s attack roll is less than your defense score, the attack misses and you avoid all damage. Your base defense is equal to 10 plus your Agility modifier and is further modified by external factors like cover.

Speed

All characters have a base speed (SPD) of 30 feet. A character gains or loses 5 feet every other modifier away from +0. For example, a character with an Agility modifier of +2 would have a speed of 35 feet, and a character with an Agility modifier of -2 would have a speed of 25 feet, and so on.

Initiative

At the beginning of every combat, you roll initiative by making an Agility check. Initiative determines the order of creatures’ turns in combat. For more information on turn order and initiative, see those headings in the Combat section in chapter 8.

Luck [LCK]

Luck measures your character’s critical attack power, as well as their chances of success in gambling or situations where no other ability score applies.

Luck Checks

A Luck check occurs when a character is outside the bounds of skill and totally within the realm of chance or fate. There are no skills that apply to Luck, but your GM might call for a Luck check when you try to accomplish tasks like the following:

  • Looting a corpse for caps and ammunition
  • Avoiding death when at 0 HP
  • Gambling at a casino
  • Avoiding being struck by lightning
Critical Strike Damage

When scoring a natural 20 on an attack roll, you get to add your critical strike damage to your attack roll. Critical strike damage is calculated by multiplying your Luck score by 2.

Saving Throws

A saving throw — also called a save — represents an attempt to resist an attack, trap, poison, disease, or a similar threat. You don’t normally decide to make a saving throw; you are forced to make one because your character or monster is at risk of harm.

To make a saving throw, roll a d20 and add the appropriate ability modifier. For example, you use your Agility modifier for an Agility saving throw. A saving throw can be modified by a situational bonus or penalty and can be affected by advantage and disadvantage, as determined by the DM.

The Difficulty Class for a saving throw is determined by the effect that causes it. For example, the DC for a saving throw from a ranged attack is determined by the shooter’s roll plus any modifier or Tag skill bonuses.

The result of a successful or failed saving throw is also detailed in the effect that allows the save. Usually, a successful save means that a creature suffers no harm, or reduced harm, from an effect.

Chapter 7: Wandering the Wastes

Whether its exploring a derelict robotics factory, investigating a mysterious town, or just wandering the wasteland, there will always be new and exciting perils and adventures.

This chapter covers the basics of the wandering life, from the mechanics of movement to the complexities of social interaction. The rules for resting are also in this chapter, along with a discussion of the activities your character might pursue between adventures.

As discussed in this guide’s introduction, the game follows a basic pattern.

  1. The GM describes the environment.
  2. The players describe what they want to do.
  3. The GM narrates the results of their actions.

Typically, the GM uses a map as an outline of the adventure, tracking the characters’ progress as they explore dungeon corridors or wasteland regions. The GM’s notes, including a map legend, describe what the survivors find as they enter each new area. Sometimes, the passage of time and the wanderers’ actions determine what happens, so the GM might use a timeline or a flowchart to track their progress instead of a map.

Time

In situations where keeping track of the passage of time is important, the GM determines the time a task requires. The GM might use a different time scale depending on the context of the situation at hand. In a dungeon environment, the adventurers’ movement happens on a scale of minutes. It takes them about a minute to creep down a long hallway, another minute to check for traps on the door at the end of the hall, and a good ten minutes to search the chamber beyond for anything interesting or valuable.

In a city or wilderness, a scale of hours is often more appropriate. The wanderers navigate through the irradiated bog in just under four hours’ time.

For long journeys, a scale of days works best. Following the road from Shady Sands to Junktown, the wanderers spend four uneventful days before a mutant ambush interrupts their journey.

In combat and other fast-paced situations, the game relies on rounds, a 10-second span of time described in chapter 9.

Movement

Wandering through a bombed-out neighborhood, swimming across an irradiated river, creaping down a dungeon corridor, scaling a craggy mountain slope - all sorts of movement play a key role in Fallout: Quest adventures. The GM can summarize the wanderers’ movement without calculating exact distances or travel times: “You travel through the petrified forest and find the dungeon entrance late in the evening of the third day.” Even in a dungeon, particularly a large dungeon or a cave network, the GM can summarize movement between encounters: “After destroying the sentry at the entrance to the abandoned brotherhood bunker, you consult your map, which leads you through miles of echoing corridors to a computer mainframe loaded with old world government secrets.” Sometimes it’s important though to know how long it takes to get from one spot to another, whether the answer is in days, hours, or minutes. The rules for determining travel time depend on two factors: the speed and travel pace of the creatures moving and the terrain they're moving over.

Speed

Every character and creature has a speed, which is the distance in feet that the character or monster can move in 1 round of combat. This number assumes short bursts of energetic movement in the midst of a life-threatening situation. The following rules determine how far a character or monster can move in a minute, an hour, or a day.

Travel Pace

While traveling, a group of adventurers can move at a normal, fast, or slow pace, as shown on the Travel Pace table. The table states how far the party can move in a period of time and whether the pace has any effect. A fast pace makes characters less perceptive, while a slow pace makes it possible to sneak around and to search an area more carefully (see the “Activity While Traveling” section later in this chapter for more information).

Forced March. The Travel Pace table assumes that characters travel for 8 hours in day. They can push on beyond that limit, at the risk of exhaustion. For each additional hour of travel beyond 8 hours, the characters cover the distance shown in the Hour column for their pace, and each character must make an Endurance saving throw at the end of the hour. The DC is 10 plus 1 for each hour past 8 hours. On a failed saving throw, a character becomes exhausted and thus rolls with disadvantage on all ability checks, attack rolls, and saving throws until their next long rest.

Difficult Terrain

The travel speeds given in the Travel Pace table assume relatively simple terrain: roads, open wasteland, or clear dungeon corridors. But adventurers often face dense forests, deep swamps, rubble-filled ruins, steep mountains, and ice-covered ground — all considered difficult terrain. You move at half speed in difficult terrain—moving 1 foot in difficult terrain costs 2 feet of speed—so you can cover only half the norm al distance in a minute, an hour, or a day.

Different Types of Movement

Movement through dangerous dungeons or wilderness areas often involves more than simply walking. Survivors might have to climb, crawl, swim, or jump to get where they need to go.

Climbing, Swimming, and Crawling

While climbing or swimming, each foot of movement costs 1 extra foot (2 extra feet in difficult terrain), unless a creature has a climbing or swimming speed. At the GM’s discretion, climbing a slippery vertical surface or one with few handholds requires a successful Strength, Endurance, or Agility check. Similarly, gaining any distance in rough water might require a different successful check.

Jumping

Your Agility determines how far you can jump.

Long Jump. When you make a long jump, you cover a number of feet up to your Agility score if you move at least 10 feet on foot immediately before the jump. When you make a standing long jump, you can leap only half that distance. Either way, each foot you clear on the jump costs a foot of movement. This rule assumes that the height of your jump doesn’t matter, such as a jump across a stream or chasm. At your GM ’s option, you must succeed on a DC 10 Agility check to clear a low obstacle (no taller than a quarter of the jump’s distance), such as a hedge or low wall. Otherwise, you hit it.

When you land in difficult terrain, you must succeed on a DC 10 Agility check to land on your feet. Otherwise, you are staggered for 1 round when you hit the ground.

High Jump. When you make a high jump, you leap into the air a number of feet equal to 3 plus your Agility modifier if you move at least 10 feet on foot immediately before the jump. When you make a standing high jump, you can jump only half that distance. Either way, each foot you clear on the jump costs a foot of movement. In some circumstances, your GM might allow you to make a check to jump higher than you normally can. You can extend your arms half your height above yourself during the jump. Thus, you can reach above you a distance equal to the height of the jump plus your 1.5x your height.

Activity While Traveling

As adventurers travel through a dungeon or the wilderness, they need to remain alert for danger, and some characters might perform other tasks to help the group’s journey.

Squad Formation

The survivors should establish a squad formation. A formation makes it easier to determine which characters are affected by traps, which ones can spot hidden enemies, and which ones are the closest to those enemies when a fight breaks out. A character might occupy the front rank, one or more middle ranks, or the back rank. Characters in the front and back ranks need enough room to travel side by side with others in their rank. When space is too tight, the marching order must change, usually by m oving characters to a middle rank.

Fewer Than Three Ranks. If a party arranges its formation with only two ranks, they are a front rank and a back rank. If there’s only one rank, it’s considered a front rank.

Stealth

While traveling at a slow pace, the characters can move stealthily. As long as they’re not visible and in the open, they can try to surprise or sneak by other creatures they encounter. See the rules for hiding in chapter 7.

Noticing Threats

Use the passive Perception scores of the characters to determine whether anyone in the group notices a hidden threat. The GM might decide that a threat can be noticed only by characters in a particular rank. For example, as the characters are exploring a maze of tunnels, the GM might decide that only those characters in the back rank have a chance to hear or spot a stealthy creature following the group, while characters in the front and middle ranks cannot.

While traveling at a fast pace, characters take a -5 penalty to their passive Perception scores to notice hidden threats.

Encountering Creatures. If the GM determines that the adventurers encounter other creatures while they’re traveling, it’s up to both groups to decide what happens next. Either group might decide to attack, initiate a conversation, run away, or wait to see what the other group does.

Surprising Foes. If the adventurers encounter a hostile creature or group, the GM determines whether the adventurers or their foes might be surprised when com bat erupts. See chapter 9 for more about surprise.

Other Activities

Characters who turn their attention to other tasks as the group travels are not focused on watching for danger. These characters don’t contribute their passive Perception scores to the group’s chance of noticing hidden threats. However, a character not watching for danger can do one of the following activities instead, or some other activity with the GM’s permission.

Navigate. You can try to prevent the group from becoming lost, making an Intelligence or Survival check when the GM calls for it.

Draw a Map. You can draw a map that records the group’s progress and helps the characters get back on course if they get lost. No ability check is required.

Track. You can follow the tracks of another creature, making a Perception or Survival check when the GM calls for it.

Scavenge. You can keep an eye out for sources of food, water, scrap, and other resources, making a Perception or Survival check when theGMcalls for it.

Radiation

Easily the most prolific and dangerous hazard in the wasteland is radiation. Radiation has trickled into nearly every nook and cranny in the of the environment at some capacity and brought about ghastly mutations in the local wildlife.

Ionizing radiation, often referred to in-game as simply radiation or rads, is the chief delayed effect of a nuclear explosion. It has long lifetimes, with half-lives ranging from days to millennia. The primary source of these products is the debris left from fission reactions. A potentially significant secondary source is neutron capture by non-radioactive isotopes both within bombs and in the outside environment.

Radiation Sources

Generally, the primary source of exposure to radiation is via irradiated food and drink. Most water sources are irradiated. Various water sources will differ in the concentration of radiation, and drinking from an irradiated source will always be more hazardous than coming into contact with the water.

Craters left by nuclear warheads are also usually areas of highly concentrated radiation.

Another primary source of radiation is from toxic waste dumped pre-War. These brown and white barrels can often be found in various locations, usually emitting low to medium levels of radiation in a close vicinity. Areas emitting higher levels in wider areas can be found at larger dump sites throughout the Fallout universe. Unlike the real world, which imposes restrictions that prevent the dumping of toxic waste, the pre-War Fallout universe seemed to have no such restrictions and dumping was not uncommon due to the high yields of toxic waste created from various forms of production and experiments.

Mutations

The ionizing radiation that has infected the wasteland has irreversibly altered the its surviving wildlife. Radiation exposure alters a living creature’s DNA thus resulting in abnormal growths or mutations. Radioactive contamination has produced an assortment of mutated creatures such as two-headed cattle, giant ants, fire-breathing geckos, ghouls, and a plethora of other sickening horrors.

Measuring and Detecting Radiation

A rad is a unit of measurement used to measure the level of radiation in an area. The rad is a real unit meaning radiation absorbed dose. It is equal to 0.01 gray. 1 gray means 1 joule of ionizing radiation was absorbed by 1 kilogram of matter, so 1 rad means 0.01 joules of radiation was absorbed by 1 kilogram of matter. In real life, although the rad is not used very often, it is still a valid scientific unit of measurement.

For every 100 rads a character is exposed to, they gain a rad-level. For the purpose of the game, we will use the term rad-level as the standard measure of radiation exposure for a character.

Radiation is undetectable to the naked eye. As described by Jake in Fallout, “Some call it the Phantom Death, 'cause that's what it is. You can't see it, you can't hear it, you can't even smell it. It basically builds up in your system. You never feel it until it's too late.” Radiation can only be measured and detected with the use of a Geiger counter, which makes a distinct ticking noise in the presence of radiation fields.

Hazards and Environmental Factors

The wasteland is naturally treacherous and filled with environmental perils in addition to the threatening flora and fauna. This section covers natural hazards of the wasteland as well as its more benign aspects.

Falling

A fall from a great height is one of the most common hazards facing a wanderer. At the end of a fall, a creature takes 1d4 damage for every 10 feet it fell. The creature is stunned for 1 round when it hits the ground, unless it avoids taking damage from the fall.

Suffocating

A creature can hold its breath for a number of minutes equal to one plus its Endurance modifier (minimum of 30 seconds). When a creature runs out of breath, it can survive for a number of rounds equal to its Endurance modifier (minimum 1 round). At the start of its next turn, it drops to 0 hit points and is dying.

Vision and Light

The most fundamental tasks of wandering — noticing danger, finding hidden objects, hitting an enemy in combat, and targeting a spell, to name just a few — rely heavily on a character’s ability to see. Darkness and other effects that obscure vision can prove a significant hindrance.

A given area might be lightly or heavily obscured. In a lightly obscured area, such as dim light, patchy fog, or moderate foliage, creatures have disadvantage on Perception checks that rely on sight.

A heavily obscured area—such as darkness, opaque fog, or dense foliage—blocks vision entirely. A creature in a heavily obscured area effectively suffers from the blinded status effect (see chapter 8).

The presence or absence of light in an environment creates three categories of illumination: bright light, dim light, and darkness.

Bright light lets most creatures see normally. Even gloomy days provide bright light, as do torches, lanterns, fires, and other sources of illumination within a specific radius.

Dim light, also called shadows, creates a lightly obscured area. An area of dim light is usually a boundary between a source of bright light, such as a torch, and surrounding darkness. The soft light of twilight and dawn also counts as dim light. A particularly brilliant full moon might bathe the land in dim light.

Darkness creates a heavily obscured area. Characters face darkness outdoors at night (even most moonlit nights), within the confines of an unlit dungeon or a subterranean vault.

Blindsight

A creature with blindsight can perceive its surroundings without relying on sight, within a specific radius. Creatures without eyes, such as oozes, and creatures with echolocation or heightened senses, such as bats and true dragons, have this sense.

Darkvision

Within a specified range, a creature with darkvision can see in darkness as if the darkness were dim light, so areas of darkness are only lightly obscured as far as that creature is concerned. However, the creature can’t discern color in darkness, only shades of gray.

Food and Water

Characters who don’t eat or drink suffer the effects of exhaustion. Exhaustion caused by lack of food or water can’t be removed until the character eats and drinks the full required amount.

Food

A character needs one pound of food per day and can make food last longer by subsisting on half rations. Eating half a pound of food in a day counts as half a day without food. A character can go without food for a number of days equal to 3 plus their Endurance modifier (minimum 1). At the end of each day beyond that limit, a character suffers from exhaustion. A norm al day of eating resets the count of days without food to zero.

Water

A character needs one gallon of water per day, or two gallons per day if the weather is hot. A character who drinks only half that much water must succeed on a DC 10 Endurance saving throw or suffer from exhaustion at the end of the day. A character with access to even less water is automatically exhausted.

Crafting

In their downtime, characters can utilize their scavenged supplies to craft various useful items and weapons. When a character has scavenged the relevant items to craft an object, they make a Repair or Survival check depending on the item in an attempt to craft it. The required materials and the DC of the check are presented in the item lists. If the check fails, the components break and are unsalvageable. Crafting an item usually takes 30 minutes to an hour to complete.

Improvised Crafting. Whenever a character wishes to craft an item that is not detailed in the item lists or wishes to substitute an item in the crafting recipe, the GM gives the required items, the relevant skill, and the required DC if it is possible.

Interacting with Objects

A character’s interaction with objects in an environment is often simple to resolve in the game. The player tells the GM what their character is doing something, such a moving a lever, and the GM describes what, if anything happens. For example, a character might decide to pull a lever, which might, in turn, raise a portcullis, cause a room to flood with water, or open a secret door in a nearby wall.

If the lever is rusted in position, though, a character might need to force it. In such a situation, the GM might call for a Strength check to see whether the character can wrench the lever into place. The GM sets the DC for any such check based on the difficulty of the task.

Characters can also damage objects with their weapons and spells. Objects are immune to poison and psychic damage, but otherwise they can be affected by physical and magical attacks much like creatures can. The GM determines an object’s hit points and might decide that certain objects have resistance or immunity to certain kinds of attacks. (It’s hard to cut a rope with a club, for example.) Objects always fail Strength and Dexterity saving throws, and they are immune to effects that require other saves. When an object drops to 0 hit points, it breaks.

A character can also attempt a Strength check to break an object. The GM sets the DC for any such check.

Social Interaction

Whether its bartering with a caravan merchant, talking down a raider gang leader, or questioning an aberrant wastelander, communication and social interaction is a crucial part of Fallout: Quest.

The GM assumes the roles of any characters who are participating in the interaction that don’t belong to another player at the table. Any such character is called a nonplayer character (NPC). In general terms, an NPC’s attitude toward you is described as friendly, indifferent, or hostile. Friendly NPCs are predisposed to help you, and hostile ones are inclined to get in your way. It’s easier to get what you want from a friendly NPC, of course. Social interactions have two primary aspects: roleplaying and ability checks.

Roleplaying

Roleplaying is, literally, the act of playing out a role. In this case, it’s you as a player determining how your character thinks, acts, and talks.

Roleplaying is a part of every aspect of the game, and it comes to the fore during social interactions. Your character’s quirks, mannerisms, and personality influence how interactions resolve.

There are two styles you can use when roleplaying your character: the descriptive approach and the active approach. Most players use a com bination of the two styles. Use whichever mix of the two works best for you.

Descriptive Approach

With this approach, you describe your character’s words and actions to the GM and the other players. Drawing on your mental image of your character, you tell everyone what your character does and how he or she does it.

When using descriptive roleplaying, keep the following things in mind:

  • Describe your character’s emotions and attitude.
  • Focus on your character’s intent and how others might perceive it.
  • Provide as much embellishment as you feel comfortable with.

Don’t worry about getting things exactly right. Just focus on thinking about what your character would do and describing what you see in your mind.

Active Approach

If descriptive roleplaying tells your GM and your fellow players what your character thinks and does, active roleplaying shows them.

When you use active roleplaying, you speak with your character’s voice, like an actor taking on a role. You might even echo your character’s movements and body language. This approach is more immersive than descriptive roleplaying, though you still need to describe things that can’t be reasonably acted out.

Results of Roleplaying

The GM uses your character’s actions and attitudes to determine how an NPC reacts. A cowardly NPC buckles under threats of violence. A stubborn mutant threatens anyone attempting to badger badger her. A vain hive mind laps up flattery.

When interacting with an NPC, pay close attention to the GM ’s portrayal of the NPC ’s mood, dialogue, and personality. You might be able to determine an NPC ’s personality traits, ideals, flaws, and bonds, then play on them to influence the NPC ’s attitude.

Interactions in the game are much like interactions in real life. If you can offer NPCs something they want, threaten them with something they fear, or play on their sympathies and goals, you can use words to get almost anything you want. On the other hand, if you insult a proud warrior or speak ill of a noble’s allies, your efforts to convince or deceive will fall short.

Ability Checks

In addition to roleplaying, ability checks are key in determining the outcome of an interaction. Your roleplaying efforts can alter an NPC’s attitude, but there might still be an element of chance in the situation. For example, your GM can call for a Charisma check at any point during an interaction if he or she wants the dice to play a role in determining an NPC ’s reactions. Other checks might be appropriate in certain situations, at your GM ’s discretion.

Resting

The toils of the wasteland beat down every survivor eventually and they will need to take time to rest. Resting is the time for tending your wounds and preparing yourself for the journey ahead.

Wanderers can take short rests in the midst of the day and a long rest at the end of the day.

Short Rest

A short rest is a period of downtime, at least 1 hour long, during which a character does nothing more strenuous than eating, drinking, reading, and tending to wounds.

A character can heal an amount of HP equal to 4d20 adding your Medicine skill to each roll.

Long Rest

A long rest is a period of extended downtime, at least 8 hours long, during which a character sleeps or perform s light activity: reading, talking, eating, or standing watch for no more than 2 hours. If the rest is interrupted by a period of strenuous activity — at least 1 hour of walking, fighting, or other adventuring activity — the characters must begin the rest again to gain any benefit from it. At the end of a long rest, a character regains all lost hit points.

A character can’t benefit from more than one long rest in a 24-hour period, and a character must have at least 1 hit point at the start of the rest to gain its benefits.

Chapter 8: Combat

This chapter provides the rules you need for your characters and monsters to engage in combat, whether it is a brief skirmish or an extended conflict in a dungeon or on a battlefield. Throughout this chapter, the rules address you, the player or Game Master. The Game Master controls all the monsters and nonplayer characters involved in combat, and each other player controls an adventurer. “You” can also mean the character or monster that you control.

Order of Combat

The game organizes the chaos of combat into a cycle of rounds and turns. A round represents about 10 seconds in the game world. During a round, each participant in a battle takes a turn. The order of turns is determined at the beginning of a combat encounter, when everyone rolls initiative. Once everyone has taken a turn, the fight continues to the next round if neither side has defeated the other.

Combat follows a general pattern:

  1. Determine Surprise: The GM determines whether anyone involved in the combat encounter is surprised.
  2. Establish Positions: The GM decides where all the characters and creatures are located. Given the survivors’ marching order or their stated positions in the room or other location, the GM figures out where the adversaries are — how far away and in what direction
  3. Roll Initiative: Everyone involved in the combat encounter rolls initiative, determining the order of combatants’ turns
  4. Take Turns: Each participant in the battle takes a turn in initiative order.
  5. Begin Next Round: When everyone involved in the combat has had a turn, the round ends. Repeat step 4 until the fighting stops.

Surprise

A band of survivors sneaks up on a mutant camp, springing from the dunes to attack them. A deathclaw creeps down a dungeon passage, unnoticed by the wanderers until the creature pounces on one of them. In these situations, one side of the battle gains surprise over the other.

The GM determines who might be surprised. If neither side tries to be stealthy, they automatically notice each other. Otherwise, the GM compares the Sneak checks of anyone hiding with the passive Perception score of each creature on the opposing side. Any character or monster that doesn’t notice a threat is surprised at the start of the encounter.

If you’re surprised, you can’t take any action on your first turn of combat other than moving, and you can’t take a reaction until that turn ends. A member of a group can be surprised even if the other members aren’t.

Initiative

Initiative determines the order of turns during combat. When combat starts, every participant makes an Agility check to determine their place in the initiative order. The GM makes one roll for an entire group of identical creatures, so each member of the group acts at the same time.

The GM ranks the combatants in order from the one with the highest Agility check total to the one with the lowest. This is the order (called the initiative order) in which they act during each round. The initiative order remains the same from round to round.

If a tie occurs, the tied characters and monsters each roll a d20 to determine the order, highest roll going first.

Characters can move their turn later in the initiative order to coordinate with their squad but cannot move their turn earlier.

Your Turn

In most cases, you have two kinds of actions to expend on your turn — a Minor action and a Major action.

Minor Action. Minor actions are smaller, less involved actions. Some Minor actions include moving, reloading your gun, or accessing an item in your inventory. You can continue making actions after your first Minor action, but your turn ends if you make a second.

Major Action. Major actions are reserved for more costly, time-consuming actions. Some Major actions include, making an attack, dashing, or changing out your armor. Your turn ends after making a Major action unless you are granted a bonus action.

Bonus Actions

Some abilities or environmental factors will give you an additional Minor or Major action called a bonus action. You can take only one bonus action on your turn, so you must choose which bonus action to use when you have more than one available.

You choose when to take a bonus action during your turn, unless the bonus action’s timing is specified, and anything that deprives you of your ability to take actions also prevents you from taking a bonus action.

Other Activity on Your Turn

Your turn can include a variety of flourishes that require neither of your actions.

You can communicate however you are able, through brief utterances and gestures, as you take your turn. You can also interact with one object or feature of the environment for free, during either your one of your actions. For example, you could open a door during your move as you stride toward a foe, or you could draw your weapon as part of the same action you use to attack.

If you want to interact with a second object, you need to use an action. The GM might require you to use an action for any of these activities when it needs special care or when it presents an unusual obstacle. For instance, the GM could reasonably expect you to use a Minor or Major action to open a stuck door or turn a crank to lower a drawbridge depending on the difficulty of the action.

Reactions

Certain special abilities, spells, and situations allow you to take a special action called a reaction. A reaction is an instant response to a trigger of some kind, which can occur on your turn or on someone else’s.

When you take a reaction, you can’t take another one until the start of your next turn. If the reaction interrupts another creature’s turn, that creature can continue its turn right after the reaction.

Movement and Position

In combat, characters and monsters are in constant motion, often using movement and position to gain the upper hand.

On your turn, you can move a distance up to your speed. You can use as much or as little of your speed as you like on your turn, following the rules here. Your movement can include jumping, climbing, and swimming. These different modes of movement can be combined with walking, or they can constitute your entire move. While you’re moving you deduct the distance of each part of your move from your speed until it is used up or until you are done moving.

Kinds of Movement Actions

On your turn, you have two kinds of movement actions available to you —the Move action and the Dash action.

Move action. The Move action allows you to move up to your speed and costs one Minor action. You can break up your Move action, making a Minor action in between intervals of movement but never a Major action. You can only make one Move action a round.

Dash action. The Dash action allows you to move up to your speed and costs you your Major action. You gain +1d4 on defense against reactions while dashing and can convert a dash action into certain perk abilities such as charge if you have them. You can only make one Dash action a round.

Using Different Speeds

If you have differing speeds for different kinds of movement, such as a walking speed and a hover speed, you can switch back and forth between your speeds during your move. Whenever you switch, subtract the distance you've already moved from the higher speed. The result determines how much farther you can move up to the maximum of the new speed. If the result is 0 or less, you can’t use the new speed during the current move.

For example, if you have a walking speed of 30 and a hovering speed of 60, you could hover 20 feet, then walk 30 feet, and then hop into the air to hover 10 feet more.

Difficult Terrain

Combat rarely takes place in bare rooms or on featureless plains. Boulder-strewn caverns, briar-choked forests, treacherous staircases — the setting of a typical fight contains difficult terrain.

Every foot of movement in difficult terrain costs 1 extra foot. This rule is true even if multiple things in a space count as difficult terrain.

Low furniture, rubble, undergrowth, steep stairs, snow, and shallow bogs are examples of difficult terrain. The space of another creature, whether hostile or not, also counts as difficult terrain.

Going Prone

Combatants can choose to go prone to gain some special advantages at the cost of their movement speed. You can drop prone without using any of your actions but standing back up takes a Minor action.

You gain +1d4 to Sneak checks while prone as well as to attack rolls and defense with and against ranged attacks respectively. Melee attack rolls have disadvantage while prone.

To move while prone, you must crawl. Every foot of movement while crawling costs 1 extra foot. Crawling 1 foot in difficult terrain, therefore, costs 3 feet of movement

Moving Around Other Creatures

You can move through a non-hostile creature’s space. In contrast, you can move through a hostile creature's space only if the creature is a size larger than you or smaller. Another creature’s space counts as difficult terrain for you regardless if they are a friend or foe. You also can’t end your move in another creature’s space even if they are your ally.

Shoving. If you attempt to pass through an enemy character’s space, they can attempt to shove you. In doing so, you enter a contest, rolling an Agility check against their Strength check. If the shove is successful, you are kept out of their space and lose any remaining movement. Otherwise you are free to continue to move your remaining speed.

Actions in Combat

When you make an action on your turn, you can take one of the actions presented here, an action you gained from a perk or weapon feature, or an action that you improvise. Many monsters have action options of their own in their stat blocks.

Access the Inventory

If you need to take a quick stimpak, swap weapons, or grab an item from your backpack, you can do so with the Access the Inventory action. You can grab and use any item in your inventory or swap your currently equipped weapons with the use of a single Minor action.

Activate a Perk

Some active perks grant you an additional ability to use in combat that isn’t necessarily a kind of Attack action. Active perks such as Moral Support, Quick Recovery, or Grim Reaper’s Sprint can be Activated to gain bonus actions or grant additional effects. An active perk’s Action Cost depends on the perk and is listed in its description in the perks section.

Attack

The most common action to take in combat is the Attack action, whether you are swinging a club, firing a shotgun blast, or brawling with a ballistics fist. With this action, you make a melee or ranged attack at the cost of your Major action. See the “Making an Attack” section for the rules that govern attacks.

Dodge

At the cost of a Major action, you can focus entirely on avoiding incoming attacks. Until the start of your next turn, you gain a bonus +1d6 defense against each attack and you make Agility saving throws with advantage. You lose this benefit if you are incapacitated or if your speed drops to 0.

Hide

At the cost of a Major action, you can attempt to Hide and regain concealment. When you make a Hide action, you make a Sneak check following the rules in chapter 7 for hiding. If you succeed, you regain concealment. You can only attempt to Hide after remaining out of a target’s line of sight for 3 or more rounds.

Improvise

Your character can do things not covered by the actions in this section, such as breaking down doors, sensing weaknesses in a foe, or doing a sweet backflip before landing an attack. The only limits to the actions you can attempt are your imagination and your character’s ability scores. See the descriptions of the ability scores in chapter 6 for inspiration as you improvise.

When you describe an action not detailed elsewhere in the rules, the GM tells you whether that action is possible, what that action costs, and what kind of roll you need to make, if any, to determine success or failure.

Ready a Minor Action

You can spend a Minor action to ready a separate Minor action as a reaction on an ally or enemy’s turn.

To do so, you decide what perceivable circumstance will trigger your reaction. Then, you choose the Minor action you will make in response to that trigger. Some examples include “If the robot steps on the trapdoor, I’ll push the button that opens it,” or “If the molerat steps next to me, I’ll move away.”

When the trigger occurs, you can either take your reaction right after the trigger finishes or ignore the trigger. Remember that you can take only one reaction per round.

Search

You can attempt to sniff out concealed foes or hidden objects with the Search action. At the cost of a Minor action, you devote your attention to finding something or someone. Depending on the nature of your search, the GM might have you make a Perception check or an Intelligence check.

Speak

At the cost of a Minor action, you can Speak to your enemies in an attempt to engage in conversation with them, either in a hope to re-establish diplomacy or break them down into surrender. As soon as you begin your Talk action, a 2-minute real-world timer begins. At the end of that timer, your turn ends, regardless if you have any remaining actions or not.

Use an Object

You normally interact with an object while doing something else, such as when you draw a pistol as part of an attack. When an object requires an action for its use, you take the Use an Object action. This action is also useful when you want to interact with more than one object on your turn. The GM will decide whether Using an Object requires a Minor or Major action depending on the object and how it is used.

Making an Attack

Whether you’re striking with a melee weapon, firing a weapon at range, or making an attack roll as part of an ability, an attack has a simple structure:

  1. Choose a target. Pick a target within your attack’s range: a creature, an object, or a location.
  2. Determine modifiers. The GM determines whether the target has cover and whether you have advantage or disadvantage against the target. In addition, Ability Scores, Tag skills, special abilities, and other effects can apply penalties or bonuses to your attack roll.
  3. Resolve the attack. You make the attack roll. On a hit, you deal your base damage plus any relevant bonus damage unless the particular attack has rules that specify otherwise. Some attacks cause special effects in addition to or instead of damage.

Attack Rolls

When you make an attack, your attack roll determines whether the attack hits or misses. To make an attack roll, roll a d20 and add the appropriate modifiers. If the total of the roll plus modifiers meets or exceeds the target’s defense (DEF), the attack hits. The defense of a character is determined by their passive Agility plus external modifiers, whereas the defense of a mutant is in its stat block.

Attack Roll Modifiers

When a character makes an attack roll, the two most common modifiers to the roll are an ability modifier and the character’s Tag skill bonus, but there are other factors that may come into play. When a monster makes an attack roll, it uses whatever modifier is provided in its stat block.

Ability Modifier. The ability modifier used for a melee weapon attack is Strength, and the ability modifier used for a ranged weapon attack is Perception. Weapons that have the finesse or thrown property break this rule.

Tag Skill Bonus. You add your Tag Skill bonus to your attack roll when you attack using a weapon with which you have a Tag skill.

Other Modifiers. You might add an attack roll modifier due to a weapon’s or ability’s accuracy bonus/penalty or due to an environmental effect such as weather or darkness.

Critical Rolls

Rolling a well-timed crit or poorly-timed crit fail on an attack can often change the tide of a battle.

Critical Success. Rolling a crit success on an attack roll makes it so your attack cannot miss and results in a critical strike, meaning that you add your critical strike damage to your attack damage.

Critical Failure. Rolling a crit fail on an attack roll results in the weapon breaking or jamming. Weapons broken due to a crit fail must be fixed through passing a DC 10 Repair check before they can be used again. Attempting to repair a weapon costs a Minor action.

Targeting Limbs

When making an attack, you can choose to aim for any one of a character’s particular limbs. Humanoid characters have 4 limb areas, the head, torso, legs, and arms.

Attacks are made to the torso by default but attacking a limb other than the torso grants the opponent a defense bonus and afflicts them with a unique hit effect depending on which one limb is hit. Hit effects last for 1 round.

Unseen Attackers and Targets

Combatants often try to escape their foes’ notice by hiding, using a stealth boy, or lurking in darkness.

Attacks made with hand-to-hand weapons such as unarmed, slash, blunt, or pierce weapons are considered melee attacks. Melee attacks may be made against any characters within your weapon’s reach.

Reach. The reach of one-handed melee weapons is 5 feet and the reach of two-handed melee weapons is 10 feet.

Range. Ranged weapons have preferred ranges depending on whether they are close-range, mid-range, or long-range weapons. Cover

Walls, trees, creatures, and other obstacles can provide cover during combat, making a target more difficult to hit with ranged attacks. A target can benefit from cover only when an attack or other effect originates on the opposite side of the cover and they had declared that they had taken that piece of cover on their previous turn. Melee attacks are unaffected by cover, but you can still apply the flanking bonus while using them.

Degrees of Cover

There are three degrees of cover. If a target is behind multiple sources of cover, only the most protective degree of cover applies; the degrees aren't added together. For example, if a target is behind a barrier that gives half cover and a tree trunk that gives full cover, the target has full cover.

Half Cover. Waist-high cover such as a sandbag pile, crumbled wall, or the hood of a car provide half cover to characters hiding behind them. You gain +4 defense against ranged attacks while behind half cover.

Full Cover. The corners of walls and other barriers as tall as your character or taller act as full cover. You can only attack out of the corners of a piece of most full cover, otherwise line of sight is blocked. You gain +8 defense against ranged attacks while behind full cover.

Character Cover. Character Cover is a variant of half cover that uses another character, whether they be friend or foe, as the piece of cover rather than a waist-high barrier or wall. If an attack roll is high enough to hit both characters, the one behind cover is hit. If an attack roll is high enough to hit the character providing the cover, but not the character taking the cover, then that character is hit. Otherwise the attack hits or misses as usual.

Flanking

If you have successfully circumnavigated around a character using a piece of cover, they are considered Flanked. Attacking flanked characters grants advantage on your attack and any damage rolls.

Damage, Healing, and Death

The battlefield is extremely dangerous; it is rare that any combatant ever leaves it completely unscathed. Thus, taking damage, healing your wounds, and avoiding death’s door is a routine part of combat.

Dealing Damage

Upon landing a hit on a target, you follow a couple of simple steps to determine how much damage you deal:

  1. Calculate the attack damage. Upon hitting a target, you start by calculating your attack damage. This number includes your weapon’s base damage modified with any relevant modifiers. Tag skill bonuses, Strength modifiers with melee weapons, Intelligence modifiers with energy weapons, etc.
  2. Subtract the DT. You then subtract your attack damage from the target’s DT. The remaining number is then dealt to their HP. Your attack damage cannot go below 0.
  3. Add bonus damage. After you deal your attack damage you then apply any relevant bonus damage. Bonus damage includes any damage dealt by dice. The bonus damage is then immediately dealt to the target’s HP, bypassing all DT.

Damage Types

Different attacks and other harmful effects deal different types of damage. Damage types have no rules of their own, but other rules, such as damage resistance, rely on the types.

The damage types follow, with examples to help a GM assign a damage type to a new effect.

Acid. Corrosive enzymes and toxic chemicals deal acid damage.

Bludgeon. Attacks from blunt weapons such as a tire iron or super sledge deal bludgeoning damage.

Cryo. Freezing weapons such as the cryolator and cryo mines deal cryo damage.

Fire. Flame attacks like a fire ant’s fire-breath or a strike from a shishkebab deal fire damage.

Force. Powerful slamming forces such as a crusher anomaly deal force damage.

Laser. Light beam attacks like the beam of a laser rifle or a protectron’s eye lasers deal laser damage.

Pierce. Puncturing and impaling attacks, such as a sharp spear or a deathclaw’s jaws deal pierce damage.

Plasma. A plasma pistol or a plasma grenade deals plasma damage.

Poison. Venomous stings and bites like a radscorpion stinger or the bite of a nightstalker deals poison damage.

Psychic. Mental abilities like an alien’s psionic blast deals psychic damage.

Pulse. EMP weapons such as pulse rifles and pulse grenades deal pulse damage.

Slash. Honed edges and blades such as swords, daggers, and claws deal slash damage.

Sonic. Sound-based attacks such as a mirelurk king’s subsonic blast deal sonic damage.

Tesla. Electric or lightning based attacks such as a stun baton or tesla cannon deal tesla damage.

Damage Resistance and Vulnerability

Some creatures and objects are exceedingly difficult or unusually easy to hurt with certain types of damage.

Resistance. If a creature or an object has resistance to a damage type, damage rolls of that type have disadvantage against it.

Vulnerability. If a creature or an object has vulnerability to a damage type, damage rolls of that type have advantage against it.

Hit Points

Hit points represent your overall physical health. Creatures with more hit points are more difficult to kill. Those with fewer hit points are more fragile.

A creature’s current hit points (usually just called hit points) can be any number from the creature’s maximum hit points down to 0. This number changes frequently as a creature takes damage or receives healing.

Whenever a creature takes damage, that damage is subtracted from its hit points. The loss of hit points has no effect on a creature’s capabilities until the creature drops to 0 hit points.

Healing

Unless it results in death, damage isn’t permanent. Rest can restore a creature's hit points (as explained in chapter 7), and aid items such as stimpaks can remove damage in an instant.

When a creature receives healing of any kind, hit points regained are added to its current hit points. A creature’s hit points can’t exceed its hit point maximum, so any hit points regained in excess of this number are lost.

Dropping to 0 Hit Points

When you drop to 0 hit points, you either die outright or fall unconscious, as explained in the following sections.

Instant Death

Massive damage can kill you instantly. When damage reduces you to 0 hit points and there is damage remaining, you die if the remaining damage equals or exceeds your maximum hit points.

Falling Unconscious

If damage reduces you to 0 hit points and fails to kill you, you fall unconscious and cannot make ANY actions. You can only regain consciousness through the use of a super stimpak or some other ability or item that specifically revives you. ends if you regain any hit points.

Death Saving Throws

Whenever you start your turn at 0 hit points, you must make a special saving throw, called a death saving throw, to determine whether you creep closer to death or hang onto life. Death saving throws are a kind of Luck saving throw.

Roll a d20 modified by your Luck score. If the roll is 10 or higher, you succeed. Otherwise, you fail. A success or failure has no effect by itself. On your third success, you become stable (see below). On your third failure, you die. The successes and failures don't need to be consecutive; keep track of both until you collect three of a kind. The number of both is reset to zero when you regain any hit points or become stable.

Critical Rolls. When you make a death saving throw and roll a crit fail, it counts as two failures. If you roll a crit success, you regain 1 hit point.

Damage at 0 Hit Points. If you take any damage while you have 0 hit points, you suffer a death saving throw failure. If the damage is from a critical hit, you suffer two failures instead. If the damage equals or exceeds your hit point maximum, you suffer instant death.

Stabilizing a Creature

The best way to save a creature with 0 hit points is to revive it. If no means of revival are available, the creature can at least be stabilized so that it isn’t killed by a failed death saving throw.

You can heal a creature above 1 HP to stabilize, otherwise you can use a Major action to administer first-aid to an unconscious creature in an attempt to stabilize it. To successfully stabilize a creature, you must pass a DC 10 Medicine check. A stable creature doesn’t make death saving throws, even though it has 0 hit points, but it does remain unconscious. The creature stops being stable, and must start making death saving throws again, if it takes any damage.

A stable creature that isn’t revived regains consciousness after 1d4 hours.

Monsters and death. Most GMs have a monster die the instant it drops to 0 hit points, rather than having it fall unconscious and make death saving throws. However, mighty villains, companions, and special NPCs are com on exceptions; the GM might have them fall unconscious and follow the same rules as player characters.

Knocking a Creature Out

Sometimes an attacker wants to incapacitate a foe, rather than deal a killing blow. When an attacker reduces a creature to 0 hit points with a melee attack, the attacker can knock the creature out. The attacker can make this choice the instant the damage is dealt. The creature falls unconscious and is stable.

Status Effects

Oftentimes a character will be inflicted with a status effect that modifies their capabilities or imposes an effect for a certain period of time. Most status effects, such as stagger, are impairments, but a few, such as invisible, can be advantageous.

A status effect lasts either until it is countered (using a bandage counters bleed for example) or for a duration of rounds specified by the effect that triggered that status.

Status effects do not stack. If you are inflicted with a status effect that you are already under the influence of, you simply reset the duration.

Any damage taken as a result of a status effect is dealt at the start of the afflicted character’s turn.

The following definitions specify what happens to a creature while it is subjected to a status effect.

Aggroed

  • You are enraged at the character that inflicted aggro and cannot attack any character other than them.

Burning

  • You take 1d8 fire damage.
  • Pass a DC 14 Endurance save or roll with disadvantage on attack rolls.
  • You can extinguish the fire by patting yourself off at the cost of a Minor action.

Bleeding

  • You take 1d6 damage.
  • Pass a DC 12 Endurance save or roll with disadvantage on attack rolls.
  • You can use a bandage to end the effect.

Blinded

  • You can’t see and automatically fail any ability check that requires sight.
  • You have disadvantage on attack rolls.

Charmed

  • You can’t attack the charmer or target them with harmful abilities.
  • The charmer has advantage on any ability check to interact socially with you.

Deafened

  • You can’t hear and automatically fail any ability check that requires hearing.

Exhausted

  • You roll with disadvantage on all ability checks, attack rolls, and saving throws.

Frightened

  • You are scared and roll with disadvantage on ability checks and attack rolls while the source of your fear is within line of sight.
  • You can’t willingly move closer to the source of your fear.

Grappled

  • See restrained…

Incapacitated

  • You cannot make any actions of any kind.
  • Attack rolls against you have advantage.

Invisible

  • You are impossible to see without a special sense (such as thermal vison). For the purpose of hiding, the you are heavily obscured.
  • Your location can be detected by any noise you make or any tracks you leave.
  • You roll with advantage on attack rolls against creatures that cannot see you.
  • Creatures that cannot see you roll with disadvantage on attack rolls against you.

Marked

  • Enemies gain +1d4 on their attack roll when making attacks against you.

Poisoned

  • You take 1d10 poison damage.
  • Pass a DC 16 Endurance save or roll with disadvantage on attack rolls.
  • Poison can be cured by consuming an antidote.

Pulsed

  • You take 1d4 pulse damage.
  • Robots, synths, power armor users, or any creature with pulse vulnerability take 1d12 pulse damage and are staggered.

Prone

  • You gain a bonus +1d4 on sneak checks and defense against ranged weapons
  • You also gain a bonus +1d4 on attack rolls with ranged weapons.
  • You attack with disadvantage with melee weapons.
  • You can only move by crawling and must use a Minor action to stand back up.

Restrained

  • Your speed is reduced to 0 and you roll with disadvantage on Agility checks.
  • You cannot perform any other actions until you escape.

Staggered

  • You lose a Minor action.

Stunned

  • You are incapacitated (see the condition), can’t move, and can speak only falteringly.
  • Attack rolls made against you have advantage.

Suppressed

  • You suffer disadvantage on attack rolls.
  • The suppressor may make an attack of opportunity on you if you enter their line of sight.

Unconscious

  • You go prone and are incapacitated.

PART 3: ITEMS & EQUIPMENT

A survivor’s gear may be the only thing standing between life and death in the day-to-day trials and tribulations of the wastes. As a wanderer continues to explore and accrue experience, they also acquire greater and more powerful items and equipment to aid them in their adventures. This section details the many, many different kinds of items that wanderers may find or encounter on their journeys and details on how to use them.

Chapter 9: Wealth

The daily trials of survival have completely rewritten the Old-World ideas of wealth. Pre-War money is seen as nothing, but worthless paper to wastelanders. Clean water, livestock, and weapons are commonly used as bartering tools in transactions between wasteland denizens and traveling caravan merchants. Caps are the primary currency in the wastes and can be used instead of bartering goods nearly universally.

Currency

It doesn’t matter if you are a savvy scientist, brainless brawler, or mal-aligned mutant, you, caps are king! Bottlecaps (or just caps for short) are the wasteland’s primary currency.

Backed by the value of water, the Hub merchants supported bottle caps because the technology to manufacture them and paint their surfaces had been mostly lost in the Great War, which limited any counterfeiting efforts. Secondly, there are a limited number of bottle caps, which preserve their value against inflation to some degree.

For similar reasons the East Coast merchants also recognize bottle caps as a currency, although who backs them and guarantees their value is unknown

Buying, Selling, and Haggling

As you continue to explore the hidden shelters and bunkers of the wasteland, you will find equipment, weapons, armor, and other treasures. You can sell the items that you do not wish to keep in towns and other settlements to wasteland merchants that are interested in what you have to offer.

Buying and Selling. You can buy and sell basic equipment with any merchant that deals in that particular stock. As a general rule, merchants will buy any item at 50% its value and sell it at 150% its value. However, most wasteland dealers will engage you in the ancient dance of haggling.

Haggling. To haggle with a merchant, you state a price and make a Barter check. If the barter check passes the required DC for the relevant buy or sell percent listed in the Marchant Haggling table, the merchant will accept the trade. If the check fails, you must raise or lower your buying or selling price respectively. In the case of a crit success on a Barter check, the merchant will immediately accept your deal. In the case of a crit fail, the merchant will buy or sell at the base price, or not at all.

Chapter 10: Armor & Helmets

The only thing standing between you and the wasteland is your armor, making it one of the most important aspects of a survivor’s arsenal. Armor provides you with your Damage Threshold (DT) as well as various unique traits depending on the armor class and the armor itself.

Damage Threshold

Armor grants you a damage threshold score, otherwise known as DT. DT directly influences the amount of damage you take by absorbing an amount of attack damage equal to your DT. Attack damage must be greater than your DT score before it starts effecting your HP directly.

Dice Damage. Although a high DT score may be able to effectively safeguard you from most attacks, it will not be able to block every bit of incoming damage. Damage dealt from dice, also known as dice damage or bonus damage, penetrates DT entirely. Meaning that although a high DT score may be able to neutralize small arms, a small percent of damage will get through even the thickest armor.

Armor Classes

There are many varieties of armor between the remaining equipment of the Pre-War and new inventions of the New-World alike. The available types of armor found in the game are separated into five categories, also known as armor classes: clothing, light armor, medium armor, heavy armor, and power armor. Each armor class has its own properties in addition to the traits of the armor itself.

Clothing

Clothing rarely supplies a DT above 10, but can provide unique boosts to ability scores, skills, and other bonuses.

Supplied DT. Clothing usually supplies DT scores between 1 and 2.

Resistances and Vulnerabilities. Clothing always has resistance against pierce damage but has vulnerability against slash damage.

Effects on Speed and Agility. Clothing is light-weight and maneuverable and thus has no effect on your speed or Agility scores unless otherwise specified.

Light Armor

Light armor is typically made of thin, flexible materials making it the armor of choice for agile survivors.

Supplied DT. Light armor usually supplies DT scores between 2 and 3.

Resistances and Vulnerabilities. Like clothing, light armor always has resistance against pierce damage and vulnerability against slash damage.

Effects on Speed and Agility. Light armor is almost never heavy enough to impose a speed or Agility penalty.

Medium Armor

Medium armor is composed of heavier materials than light armor and thus offers more protection at the cost of some maneuverability. Supplied DT. Medium armor typically supplies DT scores between 3 and 4.

Resistances and Vulnerabilities. Unlike other armor classes, medium armor has no inherent resistances or vulnerabilities.

Effects on Speed and Agility. Medium armor lowers your speed by 5 feet while wearing it and may come with additional agility penalties depending on the armor.

Heavy Armor

Heavy armor offers excellent protection at the cost of agility and movement.

Supplied DT. Heavy armor typically supplies DT scores between 4 and 5.

Resistances and Vulnerabilities. Due to its heavy construction, heavy armor has resistance against slash damage and vulnerability against pierce damage.

Effects on Speed and Agility. As the name implies, heavy armor is heavy and penalizes the user with a heavy speed penalty. Heavy armor lowers your speed by 10 feet while wearing it and usually comes with additional agility penalties depending on the armor.

Power Armor

Power armor was the pinnacle of Pre-War technology. Power armor offers the greatest defense available but is so heavy that it requires internal micro-motors to allow movement. Power armor also grants additional bonuses on top of its amazing level of protection.

Supplied DT. Power armor typically supplied DT scores between 5 and 6.

Resistances and Vulnerabilities. Like heavy armor, power armor has resistance against slash damage and vulnerability against pierce damage.

Effects on Speed and Agility. Power armor is the heaviest armor imaginable, thus reducing your speed by 15 feet while wearing it. Power armor almost always comes with additional agility penalties.

Hydraulic Actuators. Power armor is outfitted with hydraulic actuators that boost the wearers strength. You gain +2 Strength while wearing power armor and do not suffer speed penalties from heavy-duty weapons.

Kinetic Servos. Power armor is equipped with kinetic servos that are capable of absorbing over 2500 Joules of kinetic impact. You ignore 500 feet of fall damage while wearing power armor.

Hermetically Sealed. When power armor is worn alongside a power helmet, the armor is completely closed off from the outside environment. The user breathes through a respirator, thus preventing them from suffocating and giving them +3 rad resistance.

Power Armor Training. Before you can wear power armor, you must be instructed on how to use it. If you try to use power armor without power armor training, you must pass a DC 15 Science save or else the armor malfunctions and you take 1d20 damage and are stunned.

Helmets

Helmets can be worn is addition to your armor to provide bonus DT and additional effects. Helmets fall into the same classes as armor sets but provide significantly less DT. Helmets, like armor, often have traits and agility penalties of their own as well.

If your helmet has different resistances than your armor set, you only apply those resistances when your head is attacked.

Differing Armor and Helmet Classes

You can wear helmets of different class than your armor but are penalized for wearing a higher helmet class than your armor class.

For every class your helmet is above your armor set, you suffer -1 Agility. For example, if you are wearing medium armor and a heavy helmet, you suffer -1 Agility; if you are wearing clothing and a power helmet, you suffer -4 Agility.

You don’t gain any bonuses or suffer any penalties by wearing an armor set of a higher class than your helmet.

Changing Armor and Helmets

The time it takes to get in or out of armor and put on or remove a helmet is simple. It requires a Major action to equip or unequip an armor set and a Minor action to equip or unequip a helmet, regardless of its armor or helmet class.

Chapter 11: Weapons

Your weapons are your primary means of survival in skirmishes against monsters and other wasteland inhabitants. There are two main classifications of weapons, melee and ranged. The further classifications, properties, and additional features of both melee and raged weapons are broken down in the following sections.

Strength Requirements. Both melee and ranged weapons have a specific Strength requirement to use them. Using a weapon without meeting its Strength requirement results in all rolls with that weapons having disadvantage. When dual-wielding two one-handed weapons, you must to have the sum of their Strength requirements to properly wield them. The Strength requirement for each weapon is listed with its description in the item lists.

Melee Weapons

Melee weapons are used for hand-to-hand combat against enemies that are very close or right in front of you.

Melee Classes

Melee weapons are broken up into the following classes: slash, blunt, pierce, and shield.

Slash

Slash weapons include most weapons with sharp edges such as daggers, knives, and swords.

Razors-Edge. Because of their honed edge, slash weapons deal slash damage.

Blunt

Blunt weapons are weapons that deal damage by force of impact, such as a metal bat or a sledgehammer.

Impact-Force. Since blunt weapons deal damage from impact alone, they deal bludgeon damage.

Pierce

Pierce weapons have a sharpened point, such as a shiv, spear, or horn.

Pointed-Tip. Due to their sharp point, pierce weapons deal pierce damage.

Shield

Shields are personal barriers to protect yourself from harm and can be anything from a sheet of metal to a riot shield

Bonus DT and DEF. Shields grant a bonus to your overall DT and defense depending on what shield you are using. You don not benefit from your shield’s bonus DT or defense while flanked or incapacitated.

Blocking. You add your Strength modifier to your defense score when using a shield.

One-Handed. It only requires one hand to use a shield.

Damage Scaling

Most melee weapons have Strength scaling, meaning that you add your Strength modifier to your attack damage when using them. Some melee weapons are finesse weapons, meaning that they scale from Agility instead of Strength.

Ranged Weapons

Ranged weapons are used against enemies at a distance and are affected by cover and ammunition types.

Ranged Classes

Ranged weapons are broken up into the following classes: pistol, submachine gun, shotgun, assault rifle, battle rifle, light machinegun, sniper rifle, and heavy weapon.

Pistol

Pistols are semi-automatic handguns such as the .357 magnum or a pulse pistol.

Range. Pistols are close-range weapons.

Magazine Size. Pistols use small magazines. Pistols typically have 3-4 ammo-pips in their magazine with each pip representing 1 round.

One-Handed. It only requires the one hand to use a pistol.

Submachine Gun (SMG)

SMGs are single-handed automatic weapons such as an MP5k or plasmagat.

Range. SMGs are close-range weapons.

Magazine Size. SMGs use medium magazine. SMGs typically have 3-4 ammo-pips in their magazine with each pip representing 5 rounds.

One-Handed. It only requires the one hand to use an SMG.

Shotgun

Shotguns are scattershot firearms or anything that shoots shells such as a double-barrel shotgun or a plasmaspaz.

Range. Shotguns are close-range weapons.

Magazine Size. Shotguns use small magazines. Shotguns typically have 3-4 ammo-pips in their magazine with each pip representing 1 shell.

Spread. Because of their spread, shotguns deal only half their damage at long-range.

Assault Rifles (AR)

ARs are fully automatic, two-handed rifles that typically use small to mid-size calibers. Some ARs include the AUG and the tesla gun.

Range. ARs are mid-range weapons.

Magazine Size. ARs use medium magazines. ARs typically have 4-5 ammo-pips in their magazine with each pip representing 5 rounds.

Battle Rifle (BR)

BRs are single-auto, two-handed rifles that typically use mid-size to large calibers. Some BRs include the hunting rifle and the laser rifle.

Range. BRs are mid-range weapons.

Magazine Size. BRs use small magazines. BRs typically have 4-5 ammo-pips in their magazine with each pip representing 1 round.

Light Machinegun (LMG)

LMGs are large, fully-automatic weapons that use large calibers. Some LMGs include the MG43 and the minigun.

Range. LMGs are mid-range weapons.

Magazine Size. LMGs use large magazines. LMGs typically have 5-6 ammo-pips in their magazine with each pip representing 10 rounds.

Heavy-Duty. LMGs require a Major action to reload and you lose 5 feet of speed while using them.

Sniper Rifle

Snipers are long-barreled precision rifles used against long-range targets and typically use larger calibers. Some snipers include the DKS-501 and the Gauss rifle.

Range. Snipers are long-range weapons.

Magazine Size. Snipers use small magazines. Snipers typically have 3-4 ammo-pips in their magazine with each pip representing 1 round.

Heavy Weapon

Heavy weapons are massive, destructive weapons that fire heavy munitions. Some heavy weapons include the fat man and the incinerator.

Range. Heavy weapons are close-range weapons.

Magazine Size. Heavy weapons use large magazines. Heavy weapons typically have only 1 ammo-pip in their magazine with that pip representing 1 munition, but there are exceptions.

Heavy-Duty. Heavy weapons require a Major action to reload and you lose 5 feet of speed while using them.

Ranged Types

Along with the number of ranged weapon classes, there are several ranged weapon types. Ranged types determine the kind of damage a ranged weapon does and what kind of ammunition it can use in addition to various properties.

Projectile

Projectile weapons fire bullets or other solid objects toward a target to achieve damaging effects by direct application of kinetic energy. There are two kinds of projectile weapons, ballistic and gauss weapons.

Ballistic

Ballistic weapons are the most common firearms found in the wasteland. Ballistic weapons deal ballistic damage and use the standard ammunition types as well as their special variants.

Gauss

Gauss weapons are ballistic weapons that use magnetic coils to propel projectiles at devastating speeds. Gauss weapons can be spooled-up to deal +1d8 on their damage roll at the cost of a Minor action. Gauss weapons can only use 2mm EC cartridges.

Energy

Energy weapons inflict damage at a target by emission of highly focused energy. There are five kinds of energy weapons: cryo, flame, laser, plasma, and tesla weapons.

Intelligence Scaling. All energy weapons have Intelligence scaling, meaning that you add your Intelligence score to your attack damage when using them.

Cryo

Cryo weapons spray cryogenic chemicals and compounds to freeze their targets. Cryo weapons deal cryo damage and stagger their targets on hit. Cryo weapons can only use cryo cell ammunition.

Flame

Flame weapons spray streams of extremely hot, flaming liquid to engulf their enemies in fire. Flame weapons deal fire damage and set their targets on fire. Flame weapons can only use flamer fuel.

Laser

Laser weapons fire focused beams of light energy to damage their targets. Energy weapons gain +1 on attack rolls but roll with disadvantage on damage rolls and deal less bonus damage. Laser weapons deal laser damage and can use MF cells and their variants.

Plasma

Plasma weapons fire slow-moving, super-heated globs of plasma at their targets. Plasma weapons suffer -1 on attack rolls but gain advantage on damage rolls and typically deal more bonus damage. Plasma weapons deal plasma damage and can use MF cells and their variants.

Tesla

Tesla weapons fire arcs of electricity at their targets which spread to their surroundings. Tesla weapons have low attack damage but deal 1d12 damage to all targets within 15 feet which spreads to any un-hit targets within 10 feet of each other. Tesla weapons deal tesla damage and can use MF cells and their variants.

Special Ammunition

In addition to a ranged weapon’s default ammunition as defined by its class and type, there is an assortment of special munitions that can be used to bolster your attacks with unique effects. The different kinds of special ammo and their available weapon classes/types are listed in the item lists chapter.

Chapter 12: Accessories

The only thing that separates us from the animals is our ability to accessorize. You can upgrade and modify your weapons and armor with accessories to give them additional bonuses and effects.

Weapon and armor accessories can be bought from relevant merchants or found as loot in the dungeons of the wastes. You can pay a merchant 100 caps to install an accessory or try to install it yourself by passing a DC 10 Repair check, however, if you fail the check the accessory may become damaged and need to be repaired.

Some accessories do not need to be installed to be used, such as a Stealth-Boy or an eyepatch. Accessories that could reasonably be slipped on or off can be simply equipped or unequipped at the cost of a Minor action.

Armor Accessories

You might want to add a cape to your favorite set of armor or wear a bandana to hide your face. Armor accessories allow you to add personal flair to your helmets and armor and grant bonus effects to them. Armor accessories fall under two categories: armor set accessories and face accessories.

Conflicting Slots. Armor accessories can be worn on various parts of the body, but you cannot have two armor accessories occupying the same place. For example, if you have a Pip-Boy 3000 on your wrist, you could not then wear a gauntlet or watch on that same wrist.

Close-Faced Helmets. Face accessories can only be worn in conjunction with open-faced helmets. Helmets are open-faced by default, but other helmets such as a welder’s mask or a power helmet are close-faced and thus cannot be worn alongside face accessories.

Weapon Mods

Your weapons of choice can be outfitted with upgrades called weapon mods. There are various weapon modifications for both melee and ranged weapons, however, there are differing rules on how many and where weapon mods can be attached depending on which kind of weapon you are using.

Melee Weapons. Melee weapons can only have two weapon mods at a time: one on the grip and one on the weapon itself. Melee weapon mods must also be installed before gaining their bonuses.

Ranged Weapons. Ranged weapons can have mods on various parts of the weapon. For most ranged weapons there are three mod slots: the upper-rail, the under-rail, and the magazine. Ranged weapon mods are easier to attach, and thus can be equipped/unequipped with the use of a Minor action.

Chapter 13: Combat Tools

In addition to your primary weapons you have many additional tools of destruction at your fingertips. You can use thrown weapons, grenades, and mines to deal damage and disrupt your enemies. The details of each type of combat tool is laid out in this chapter.

Action Cost. It only costs a Minor action to use a combat tool and can be combined with the Access the Inventory action.

Range. Combat tools can be thrown as far as your throwing distance (5 x Strength ft.) and are considered close-range weapons.

Thrown Weapons

Throwing weapons can be used to attack or disrupt targets that are at a distance and can pose as deadly ranged tools for melee characters.

Attack Rolls. Making an attack with a thrown weapon behaves similarly to making an attack with a ranged weapon, except instead of using your Perception modifier on the attack roll, you use your Strength modifier.

Damage Scaling. Thrown weapons that hit a target directly have Strength scaling (or Agility scaling if it’s a finesse weapon).

Grenades

Grenades are useful for displacing enemy units or dealing large amounts of damage over a large area of effect.

Uncooked Grenades. Uncooked grenades can be thrown anywhere within your throwing distance and explode 1 round after they are thrown.

Cooking Grenades. If you wish to have a grenade explode as soon as it lands on its target, you will need to cook it first. To cook a grenade, you make a DC 10 Explosives check. If you fail, the grenade blows up prematurely in the air and deals no damage. Cooking a grenade costs an additional Minor action.

Throwing Back. You can throw back uncooked grenades at the cost of a Minor action but must pass a DC 20 Explosives check. If you fail, the grenade explodes in your hands.

Damage Radius. Grenades deal their base damage in an area of effect defined by their damage radius.

Mines

Mines can be used to block off pathways and useful avenues of movement for your enemies or spring a deadly trap.

Motion Sensors. Mines have motion sensors that activate 1 round after they are placed or thrown. Any character that moves within the sensor radius detonates the mine when they are active.

Damage Radius. Mines deal their base damage in an area of effect defined by their damage radius, which is typically the same distance as their sensor radius.

Hiding Mines. Mines can be hidden in deep brush or in the ground using a tool such as a shovel. When hiding a mine, you make an Explosives check that must be beaten by an opposing characters passive Perception when crossing into the mine’s sensor radius or by a Perception check via the Search action to be detected. Hiding a mine costs a Minor action and delays the activation of the motion sensors by 1 round.

Chapter 14: Consumables

Sometimes you might need a helping hand in the middle of a firefight. Luckily there are an assortment of different consumables at your disposal to replenish your health, alleviate status effects, and give you useful buffs to help you vanquish your foes. The details of aid, chems, and food items, useful for any survivor braving the wasteland, are described in this chapter.

Aid

Aid items such as bandages and stimpaks are useful when you are afflicted with status effects or wounded.

Medicine Skill. Your medicine skill is added to any hit points regained using aid items. If the aid item calls for multiple rolls of a die or multiple dice, you only add your Medicine skill to one.

Chems

There is a wide variety of drugs, otherwise known as chems, that you can take to modify your ability scores and receive unique effects. chems are extremely useful in the heat of battle but should only be used in moderation due to their addictive properties.

Chem duration. The effects of chems last for multiple rounds. The duration of a chems effects are listed in its description.

Addiction Saves. Whenever you take a chem, you must pass an Endurance save called an addiction save to avoid becoming addicted to that chem. The roll required to avoid addiction is different for each chem and is listed in it description. Taking the same chem twice within a 24-hour period will force you to roll with disadvantage on your addiction save, and you always roll with disadvantage on addiction saves against chems that you have been previously addicted to.

Effects of Addiction. When suffering from addiction, you roll with disadvantage on all rolls when not under the influence of that chem.

Curing Addiction. Chem addiction can only be cured in two ways: visiting a doctor or using fixer (another chem).

Food

In addition to aid items, you can consume indigenous wasteland flora and fauna or packaged foodstuffs from the Pre-War to regain lost hit points. Most food found in the wasteland is contaminated with radiation, so beware before you bite.

Rads. Different food items inflict different levels of radiation. The amount of rads taken by ingesting a particular food item is listed in its description.
